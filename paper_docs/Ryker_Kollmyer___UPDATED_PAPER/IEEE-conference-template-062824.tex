\begin{abstract}
%=========================================================================
This paper introduces the Resilient Election and Impeachment Policy
(REIP), a distributed governance framework that enables multi-robot
teams to detect, impeach, and replace compromised leaders without
centralized oversight.  Each robot maintains a trust ledger and verifies
leader commands \emph{before execution} using a three-tier
confidence-weighted model: personal visit history (ground truth), sensor
readings (physically verifiable), and peer-reported coverage (lower
confidence).  This proactive approach yields a closed-form worst-case
detection bound of $\lceil \sigma / (w_{\min} - r) \rceil$
commands---2~under ground-truth evidence, 8~under peer reports.  In
multiroom simulation with dual sequential Byzantine fault injection
($N{=}100$ trials per condition), REIP achieves 100\% median
coverage under both clean and adversarial conditions, with median
first suspicion in $0.20{-}0.21\,$s and impeachment in
$1.21{-}1.67\,$s across fault types---a
$+24.5\,$pp mean coverage advantage over Raft consensus under
Byzantine Assignment and $+32.2\,$pp under Freeze Leader, which
cannot detect a leader that misbehaves while maintaining heartbeats.
An ablation study confirms that removing the trust model collapses
coverage to $39.7\%$, demonstrating that trust is the core mechanism.
Hardware validation on a five-robot embedded platform confirms
sub-second fault detection.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
multi-agent systems, frontier exploration, trust governance, resilient
robotics, autonomous swarms, distributed fault detection
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
%=========================================================================

\subsection{Why Multi-Agent Systems Matter}
Autonomous multi-robot teams are rapidly moving from laboratory curiosity
to operational necessity.  NASA's proposed multi-robot missions for
mapping lunar lava tubes envision teams of rovers that must explore
GPS-denied underground environments without real-time human
oversight~\cite{whittaker2012lunar}.  The DARPA Subterranean Challenge
demonstrated that coordinated multi-robot teams consistently outperformed
single platforms in underground search-and-rescue
mapping~\cite{chung2023darpa}.  In terrestrial industry, autonomous
mining operations increasingly deploy fleets of coordinated vehicles that
must continue productive work even when individual machines
fail~\cite{marshall2016robotics}.  Search-and-rescue scenarios after
structural collapses demand rapid, coordinated coverage of rubble fields
where human entry is too dangerous~\cite{murphy2004rescue}.

The fundamental advantage is scaling: $N$ robots can, in principle, cover
$N$~times more area than a single platform.  But realizing this advantage
requires \emph{coordination}---without it, agents redundantly explore the
same areas, collide, or leave critical regions uncovered.

\subsection{The Importance of Leadership}
Consider an orchestra.  Thirty musicians each possess individual skill,
but without a conductor to assign parts, set tempo, and resolve
conflicts, the result is cacophony---not a symphony.  The conductor does
not play an instrument; the conductor's role is to organize \emph{who
plays what and when}.  Multi-robot exploration faces an analogous
coordination problem: a designated leader can allocate frontier targets,
prevent duplication, and exploit a global view of the team's coverage
state, yielding substantially higher task efficiency than purely
decentralized approaches~\cite{burgard2000collaborative,
simmons2000coordination}.

Critically, this architecture also provides a \emph{computational}
advantage: the leader absorbs the cost of global map fusion and frontier
allocation while followers perform only local map merging, command
execution, and trust verification.  On resource-constrained platforms
such as the Raspberry Pi Zero~2W used in this work, concentrating the
most expensive operations on a single elected node keeps per-agent
compute lightweight and information flow structured---followers report
coverage, the leader computes, the leader broadcasts.

This leader--follower architecture is widely adopted precisely because it
works well when the leader is functioning
correctly~\cite{yamauchi1997frontier}.  But it introduces a critical
vulnerability.

\subsection{The Single Point of Failure}
Unlike a crashed process that simply stops (and is easily detected), a
\emph{subtly compromised} leader can remain alive, maintain heartbeats,
and continue issuing commands---commands that are degraded, adversarial,
or nonsensical.  Sensor faults, adversarial interference, and
AI-generated hallucinations can all produce a leader that appears healthy
to liveness checks while silently dragging the entire team's performance
toward failure~\cite{douceur2002sybil, ji2023hallucination}.

This failure mode is not hypothetical.  In physical robotic deployments,
sensor drift, dust occlusion, and communication corruption are common.
A leader whose camera is occluded may confidently assign targets in
already-explored regions; a leader under adversarial attack may
deliberately waste followers' energy.  In both cases, the team follows
bad orders because no mechanism exists to \emph{evaluate the quality} of
those orders.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Existing approaches do not solve this problem.  Classical Byzantine
Fault-Tolerant (BFT) protocols assume reliable all-to-all communication
and honest supermajorities that are rarely achievable in range-limited
robotic teams~\cite{lamport1982byzantine, castro1999practical}.
Consensus algorithms such as Raft detect leader failure only via heartbeat
timeout~\cite{ongaro2014raft}---a leader that misbehaves while
maintaining heartbeats is invisible to Raft.  Frontier-based exploration
strategies assume agents act in good faith~\cite{yamauchi1997frontier,
burgard2000collaborative}, and existing trust
frameworks~\cite{marsh1994formalising} are seldom coupled with concrete
impeachment-and-replacement policies for robotic teams.

A further gap separates simulation-validated governance from real-world
deployment.  Reactive trust models that measure performance \emph{after}
commands are followed introduce detection latency that wastes physical
time and energy~\cite{chandola2009anomaly}.  Real-time deployment demands
\emph{proactive} verification---agents must evaluate leader commands
\emph{before executing} them.

\subsection{Proposed Solution}
This paper proposes the \textbf{Resilient Election and Impeachment Policy
(REIP)}, a trust-based governance framework for distributed multi-agent
systems.  REIP combines per-agent fault detection, democratic leader
impeachment, and merit-based leader election without requiring
centralized oversight or ground-truth oracle access.

The core mechanism is a \emph{proactive} trust model: each robot verifies
leader commands against local knowledge \emph{before execution} using
three-tier confidence-weighted suspicion accumulation.  Evidence is ranked
by source reliability---personal experience outweighs sensor readings,
which outweigh peer reports---enabling fast detection from strong evidence
while preventing false positives from stale data.

Returning to the orchestra analogy: REIP gives each musician the ability
to independently judge whether the conductor's directions make musical
sense.  If enough musicians independently conclude the conductor is
calling wrong notes, they vote to replace the conductor mid-performance
---without stopping the music.

An earlier reactive model using CUSUM--Bayesian fault
detection~\cite{page1954cusum, gelman2013bayesian} was validated across
2\,000 GridWorld trials, demonstrating a 76~percentage point resilience
advantage.  However, reactive detection requires following bad commands
before measuring their damage---a cost unacceptable in physical
deployment.  This motivated the proactive model described above, which
runs identical code in both simulation (Section~\ref{sec:hw_sim}) and
on embedded hardware (Raspberry Pi Zero~2W), achieving sub-second fault
detection without executing bad commands.

\subsection{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item A trust-based governance framework enabling distributed fault
    detection, democratic impeachment, and merit-based leader election
    in multi-agent exploration without ground-truth oracle access.
    \item A proactive three-tier confidence-weighted trust model that
    verifies commands \emph{before execution} against local knowledge
    ranked by source reliability, with a closed-form worst-case
    detection bound.
    \item A causality-aware verification mechanism that prevents false
    positives from concurrent exploration by timestamping visited cells
    against leader assignment times.
    \item Rigorous validation with $N{=}100$ trials per condition,
    comparing REIP against Raft consensus and decentralized
    baselines under dual sequential Byzantine fault injection, plus
    a component ablation study quantifying the contribution of each
    module.
    \item Hardware validation on a five-robot embedded platform with
    overhead ArUco-based localization~\cite{garrido2014aruco}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
%=========================================================================

REIP lies at the intersection of multi-robot coordination for extreme
environments, Byzantine fault tolerance, frontier-based exploration, and
computational trust.  We review each area, emphasizing the gap between
existing capabilities and the demands of real-world deployment.

\subsection{Multi-Robot Systems in High-Stakes Applications}
Multi-robot coordination has moved from theory to practice in
environments where single-agent approaches are insufficient.  The DARPA
Subterranean Challenge showed that heterogeneous robot teams achieved the
highest mapping scores in underground tunnel, urban, and cave
circuits~\cite{chung2023darpa}, confirming that coordinated multi-robot
exploration outperforms single-platform approaches in complex,
GPS-denied environments.  NASA has proposed multi-robot missions for
exploring lunar lava tubes and Martian caves, where communication
blackouts and terrain complexity make centralized human control
infeasible~\cite{whittaker2012lunar}.  In the mining industry,
autonomous haul trucks and drill rigs already operate in coordinated
fleets; when one vehicle's planner degrades, the fleet's productivity
drops proportionally unless the system can detect and compensate for the
failure~\cite{marshall2016robotics}.  Post-disaster search-and-rescue
teams deploy multiple robots to rapidly cover collapsed structures too
dangerous for human entry~\cite{murphy2004rescue}.

In each of these domains, the cost of a coordination failure is measured
in lost mission time, wasted energy, or---in search-and-rescue---lives.
This motivates the need not just for multi-robot coordination, but for
\emph{resilient} coordination that can survive the failure of any
individual component, including the coordinator itself.

\subsection{Byzantine Fault Tolerance and Distributed Governance}
Classical BFT protocols provide strong guarantees against arbitrary faults
under assumptions of reliable communication and honest
supermajorities~\cite{lamport1982byzantine}.  Practical protocols such as
PBFT relax some synchrony assumptions~\cite{castro1999practical}, and
consensus algorithms such as Raft provide leader election and log
replication for crash-tolerant distributed
systems~\cite{ongaro2014raft}.  However, these approaches typically
assume dense communication graphs or reliable message delivery that are
difficult to realize in range-limited robot teams.

REIP adopts the spirit of BFT---explicitly modeling faulty leaders and
governing their authority---but operates under partial observability and
lossy communication.  Unlike Raft, which detects leader failure only via
heartbeat timeout~\cite{ongaro2014raft}, REIP incorporates
\emph{task-level performance reasoning} into its leader assessment.
This distinction is critical: a leader that is alive and communicating
but issuing degraded commands (e.g., from sensor hallucination or
adversarial compromise) will never trigger Raft's timeout-based
detection, leaving the team under indefinite faulty leadership.

\subsection{Multi-Robot Frontier Exploration}
Frontier-based exploration assigns robots to boundaries between known and
unknown space~\cite{yamauchi1997frontier}, with multi-robot extensions
coordinating assignments to maximize coverage
rate~\cite{burgard2000collaborative}.  Leader--follower architectures
exploit a global view for coordinated
assignment~\cite{simmons2000coordination}, but treat the leader as
implicitly trustworthy---analogous to an orchestra that follows the
conductor's baton without question.  There is no mechanism for detecting
when the leader's directions become counterproductive, nor for replacing
the leader without external supervision.  REIP augments a standard
frontier-based leader--follower stack with a governance layer that
monitors, impeaches, and replaces a failing leader based on observed
performance---giving each agent the autonomy to question orders and,
collectively, to act on that judgment.

\subsection{Trust and Reputation in Multi-Agent Systems}
Computational trust frameworks quantify agent reliability from observed
behavior~\cite{marsh1994formalising}, and have been applied in
peer-to-peer networks, e-commerce, and multi-agent
simulations~\cite{ramchurn2004trust}.  However, most trust models assume
access to ground-truth outcomes: a buyer can verify whether the product
was delivered correctly.  In multi-robot exploration under partial
observability, ground truth is not directly available; agents observe only
local coverage changes and peer broadcasts.

REIP implements a belief-only trust ledger that operates without an
oracle map or centralized evaluator.  Rather than measuring trust from
\emph{outcomes} (which require following potentially bad commands), the
proactive model verifies leader commands against local knowledge
\emph{before execution} (Section~\ref{sec:hw_trust}), making it
suitable for both simulation and resource-constrained hardware.

\subsection{Proactive vs.\ Reactive Fault Detection}
Most fault detection in multi-agent systems is
reactive~\cite{chandola2009anomaly}: anomalies are detected after
performance degrades.  Residual-based detectors monitor prediction errors
post-execution, introducing latency between fault onset and detection.
In physical robotic systems, this latency translates directly to wasted
movement, energy, and mission time---resources that are strictly limited
in space exploration, underground mining, and disaster response.

Proactive approaches that verify commands \emph{before} execution are
rare in multi-robot governance literature.  REIP's proactive trust model
contributes such a mechanism: agents check leader commands against
locally verifiable knowledge before committing to execution, ensuring
that bad commands are flagged before they consume physical resources.

\section{System Architecture}
%=========================================================================

REIP operates as a governance layer atop a frontier-based leader--follower
exploration stack.  We describe both simulation and hardware
architectures.

\subsection{Layered Design}
The system operates on three layers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Agent Knowledge Layer}: Each agent maintains a
    high-resolution local map of personally observed cells, a
    low-resolution global belief map aggregated from peer broadcasts, and
    a coverage/motion history.
    \item \textbf{Communication Layer}: Agents share compressed belief
    updates within communication range via peer-to-peer broadcast.
    \item \textbf{Governance Layer}: A distributed trust ledger and
    democratic impeachment/election protocol that determine leadership.
\end{enumerate}

The distinction between local and global maps is central to the hardware
trust model (Section~\ref{sec:hw_trust}): agents treat personal
observations as ground truth and peer-reported data as lower-confidence
evidence.

\subsection{Simulation Environment}
A physics-based harness simulates a $2000 \times 1500\,$mm arena with
$125\,$mm grid cells ($16 \times 12 = 192$ cells) and interior wall
segments (Section~\ref{sec:hw_sim}).  Robot processes run the full
REIP/Raft/Decentralized node software and communicate via UDP---identical
to hardware deployment.  Time-based physics ensure consistent movement
regardless of CPU load, with sub-step collision detection against walls.
An earlier GridWorld simulation ($60 \times 60$ cells, 2\,000 seeds)
validated the reactive trust model; those results are summarized in
Section~\ref{sec:sim_trust} and detailed in the supplemental repository.

\subsection{Hardware Platform}
\label{sec:hw_arch}
The hardware platform consists of five custom-built differential-drive
robots ($147 \times 111 \times 42\,$mm, $86.5\,$mm wheelbase) operating
in a $2000 \times 1500\,$mm arena partitioned into a virtual grid of
$125\,$mm cells ($16 \times 12 = 192$ cells).  Each robot is built
around a Raspberry Pi Zero 2W running the REIP node software, with a
Raspberry Pi Pico handling low-level motor control via UART.  Drive is
provided by two N20 geared DC motors (1:100 ratio) with magnetic
encoders, and a rear ball caster for stability.  Each robot carries five
VL53L0X time-of-flight (ToF) sensors on a TCA9548A I2C multiplexer,
providing $200\,$mm range obstacle detection.

Localization is provided by an overhead camera (Logitech C922 at 1080p,

solely as a localization service: it detects each robot's ArUco marker,
computes pose $(x, y, \theta)$ in arena coordinates via a four-corner
homography transform using dedicated corner markers at the arena
boundaries, and transmits each robot's position via UDP broadcast.  The
PC has no knowledge of coverage state, leadership, trust, or task
assignments.

All coordination is fully distributed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Robots broadcast state to peers via UDP at 5\,Hz;
    \item Each robot maintains its own visited-cell set and merges peer
    broadcasts into a shared coverage belief;
    \item Leadership election, trust assessment, and impeachment execute
    locally on each robot;
    \item The leader computes and broadcasts frontier assignments;
    followers verify assignments using the proactive three-tier trust
    model (Section~\ref{sec:hw_trust}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{REIP Controller Decomposition}
The REIP controller comprises two complementary submodules:

\noindent\textbf{Governance} maintains the three-tier trust assessment
module, merit-based leader election, democratic impeachment, and peer
anomaly detection that monitors non-leader agents for behavioral faults
(spinning, stuck, coverage stall) to prevent compromised agents from
inheriting leadership.

\noindent\textbf{Governed fallback} allows followers to revert to local
frontier exploration when trust in the leader drops below a threshold.
Governance determines \emph{when} to fall back; fallback provides the
safe behavior \emph{during} leadership transitions.  Without governance,
agents have no principled mechanism for deciding when to ignore the
leader.  Without fallback, governance can detect faults but agents have
no safe behavior to execute.  The two components are complementary:
governance is the trigger, fallback is the safety net.

\section{REIP Methodology}
%=========================================================================

\subsection{Reactive Origins}
\label{sec:sim_trust}

REIP's development began with a reactive trust model validated across
2\,000 seeded GridWorld trials.  That model monitored coverage prediction
error using hybrid CUSUM--Bayesian fault
detection~\cite{page1954cusum, gelman2013bayesian}: followers compared
predicted coverage gain against observed gain, and statistical detectors
flagged sustained discrepancies.  Under adversarial conditions, the
reactive model raised coverage from 22.0\% (baseline) to 98.0\%---a
76~percentage point resilience advantage.  An ablation study confirmed
that governance (detection + impeachment) and fallback autonomy
(local exploration during transitions) are complementary: removing either
component alone collapsed resilience to ${\sim}14\%$ success rate.

However, reactive detection requires \emph{executing} bad commands before
measuring their damage.  In continuous-time physical environments, each
bad command wastes movement, battery, and mission time.  This fundamental
limitation motivated the proactive model described below, which verifies
commands \emph{before execution}.

%---------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Proactive Trust Model: Three-Tier Verification}
\label{sec:hw_trust}

The proactive trust model verifies leader commands \emph{before
execution}, eliminating the physical cost of following bad commands.
This is the trust model used in all multiroom and hardware experiments;
it runs identical code on simulation processes and Raspberry Pi
Zero~2W nodes.

\subsubsection{Information Zones and Verification}
Each robot maintains two overlapping information zones that determine
what it can verify about a leader's command:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-resolution zone}: cells the robot has personally
    visited.  This is absolute ground truth regardless of the robot's
    current position or distance from those cells---the memory of having
    been there is permanent.
    \item \textbf{Sensor zone}: cells currently within ToF range
    ($200\,$mm).  Obstacle readings here are instantaneous physical
    measurements that are regenerated each cycle and do not accumulate.
\end{itemize}

Beyond these zones, the robot relies on the low-resolution global belief
map built from peer broadcasts.  This data may be stale: a cell reported
as unexplored 2 seconds ago may have been visited by another robot since
the last broadcast.  The three-tier model weights evidence accordingly.

\subsubsection{Confidence-Weighted Suspicion}
When the leader assigns a target cell, the follower checks it against
three tiers of local knowledge ranked by reliability:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Tier~1 --- Personal visit history} ($w_1 = 1.0$):
    The assigned cell is in the robot's own visited set.  This is ground
    truth; the command is provably wasteful.
    \item \textbf{Tier~2 --- ToF obstacle detection} ($w_2 = 1.0$):
    The assigned cell is within current ToF range and contains a detected
    obstacle.  This is directly and physically verifiable.
    \item \textbf{Tier~3 --- Peer-reported coverage} ($w_3 = 0.3$):
    The assigned cell appears in the merged belief map from peer
    broadcasts but not in the robot's own visited set.  This evidence
    may be stale.
\end{enumerate}

Suspicion accumulates when a bad command is detected and decays slowly
for good commands:
\begin{equation}
    S(t{+}1) = \begin{cases}
        S(t) + w_{\text{tier}} & \text{if command flagged} \\
        \max\bigl(0,\; S(t) - r\bigr) & \text{if command is good}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:suspicion}
\end{equation}
where $r = 0.1$ is the recovery rate, set strictly below $w_3 = 0.3$ to
ensure that a leader alternating good and bad commands cannot wash out
accumulated suspicion.

When $S$ exceeds threshold $\sigma = 1.5$:
\begin{equation}
    T_{\text{leader}} \leftarrow \max\bigl(T_{\min},\;
    T_{\text{leader}} - \Delta_{\text{decay}}\bigr),
    \quad S \leftarrow S - \sigma
    \label{eq:trust_step}
\end{equation}
The carry-over subtraction ($S \leftarrow S - \sigma$ rather than
$S \leftarrow 0$) ensures that a persistently bad leader faces
accelerating trust decay: residual suspicion from one threshold crossing
carries into the next evaluation window.

\subsubsection{Worst-Case Detection Bound}
\label{sec:detection_bound}
The tiered weighting produces a closed-form worst-case detection bound.
Under persistent bad commands, suspicion grows by $w_{\text{tier}} - r$
per command (net of recovery decay on intervening good commands in the
worst case).  The number of commands to the first trust decay is:
\begin{equation}
    n_{\text{detect}} = \left\lceil \frac{\sigma}{w_{\text{tier}} - r}
    \right\rceil
    \label{eq:detection_bound}
\end{equation}
Under Tier~1 evidence ($w_1 = 1.0$, $r = 0.1$):
$n_{\text{detect}} = \lceil 1.5 / 0.9 \rceil = 2$ commands.  Under
Tier~3 evidence only ($w_3 = 0.3$, $r = 0.1$):
$n_{\text{detect}} = \lceil 1.5 / 0.2 \rceil = 8$ commands.  This
eight-fold difference directly reflects the epistemological gap between
ground-truth and peer-reported evidence, and prevents false impeachments
from stale peer data while maintaining fast detection from
high-confidence personal knowledge.

Full impeachment requires trust to decay from $T_0 = 1.0$ to
$\tau_{\text{imp}} = 0.3$.  Each threshold crossing decrements trust by
$\Delta_{\text{decay}} = 0.2$, so the number of crossings needed is:
\begin{equation}
    k_{\text{imp}} = \left\lceil \frac{T_0 - \tau_{\text{imp}}}
    {\Delta_{\text{decay}}} \right\rceil = \left\lceil \frac{0.7}{0.2}
    \right\rceil = 4
    \label{eq:impeach_crossings}
\end{equation}
The total worst-case commands to impeachment is $k_{\text{imp}} \times
n_{\text{detect}}$:
\begin{equation}
    n_{\text{imp}} = \left\lceil \frac{T_0 - \tau_{\text{imp}}}
    {\Delta_{\text{decay}}} \right\rceil \cdot
    \left\lceil \frac{\sigma}{w_{\text{tier}} - r} \right\rceil
    \label{eq:impeachment_bound}
\end{equation}
yielding 8~commands under Tier~1 evidence ($w_1 = 1.0$) or 32~commands
under Tier~3 evidence alone ($w_3 = 0.3$).

\subsubsection{Causality-Aware Verification}
A potential source of false positives is concurrent exploration: a cell
may be visited by a peer \emph{after} the leader computed its assignment
but \emph{before} the follower verifies the command.  To prevent this,
each robot timestamps cells when visited and records the time of each
leader assignment.  A cell triggers suspicion only if it was known to be
explored \emph{well before} the leader's assignment was sent, accounting
for broadcast propagation delay~$\delta_b$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{flag}(c) = \mathbb{I}\bigl(c \in \texttt{known\_visited}
    \;\wedge\; t_{\text{known}}(c) < t_{\text{assign}} - \delta_b\bigr)
\end{equation}
where $\delta_b = 0.5\,$s (approximately $2.5\times$ the broadcast
period at $5\,$Hz).  This grace period reflects the inherent information
delay in the distributed system: if a cell was explored less than
$\delta_b$ seconds before the assignment, the leader's merged map may
not yet contain the update.  The check applies to all three tiers,
eliminating false positives from network latency and asynchronous map
updates without weakening detection of genuinely bad commands---a
compromised leader sends robots to cells explored \emph{long} before the
assignment, well outside the grace window.

\subsubsection{Direction Consistency Verification}
The three-tier model verifies commands against known map state.  A
complementary check compares the leader's commanded direction against
the follower's belief about where unexplored territory lies.  The
follower computes the angular difference between the leader's assigned
target and \emph{every} unexplored cell in its belief map, retaining
the best (smallest) alignment:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \bigl|\text{atan2}(\Delta y_f,
    \Delta x_f) - \text{atan2}(\Delta y_{\text{cmd}},
    \Delta x_{\text{cmd}})\bigr|
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{F}$ is the set of unexplored frontier cells, all
angles are normalized to $[0, \pi]$, and $\Delta y_f, \Delta x_f$ are
relative to the follower's current position.  If
$\alpha > 3\pi/4$ (135$^\circ$), the leader's command points away from
\emph{every} known frontier, and direction-consistency suspicion is
added independently.  For $\pi/2 < \alpha \leq 3\pi/4$, direction
suspicion is added only when the three-tier check has already flagged
the command, acting as a reinforcing signal rather than a standalone
trigger.  This prevents false positives when the leader assigns a
specific distant frontier that diverges from the follower's nearest-cell
heuristic, while still catching severe directional faults.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:three_tier} summarizes the complete per-command
verification procedure including causality checks and direction
consistency verification.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Three-Tier Command Verification}
\label{alg:three_tier}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Leader-assigned target cell $c$, assignment time $t_a$, state
\STATE $w \leftarrow 0$
\STATE \COMMENT{\textbf{--- Three-Tier Check (causality-gated) ---}}
\IF{$c \in \texttt{my\_visited}$ \AND $t_{\text{visit}}(c) < t_a - \delta_b$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 1.0$
    \COMMENT{Tier 1: personal ground truth}
\ELSIF{$c$ within ToF range \AND obstacle at $c$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 1.0$
    \COMMENT{Tier 2: sensor verification}
\ELSIF{$c \in \texttt{known\_visited} \setminus \texttt{my\_visited}$
       \AND $t_{\text{known}}(c) < t_a - \delta_b$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 0.3$
    \COMMENT{Tier 3: peer-reported (lower confidence)}
\ENDIF
\STATE \COMMENT{\textbf{--- Direction Consistency ---}}
\STATE $\alpha \leftarrow \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}}
       \lvert\theta_f - \theta_c\rvert$
       \COMMENT{Best alignment with any frontier}
\IF{$\alpha > 3\pi/4$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow w + 0.5 + 0.3 \cdot
           \frac{\alpha - 3\pi/4}{\pi/4}$
    \COMMENT{Severe: away from all frontiers}
\ELSIF{$\alpha > \pi/2$ \AND $w > 0$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow w + 0.3 \cdot
           \frac{\alpha - \pi/2}{\pi/4}$
    \COMMENT{Reinforcing signal only}
\ENDIF
\STATE \COMMENT{\textbf{--- Suspicion Update ---}}
\IF{$w > 0$}
    \STATE $S \leftarrow S + w$
\ELSE
    \STATE $S \leftarrow \max(0,\; S - r)$
    \COMMENT{Decay toward zero}
\ENDIF
\WHILE{$S \geq \sigma$}
    \STATE $T_{\text{leader}} \leftarrow \max(T_{\min},\;
           T_{\text{leader}} - \Delta_{\text{decay}})$;
           $S \leftarrow S - \sigma$
\ENDWHILE
\IF{$T_{\text{leader}} < \tau_{\text{imp}}$}
    \STATE Trigger impeachment vote
\ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Design Rationale}
The proactive model was chosen over a reactive or hybrid design for three
reasons: (1)~at hardware scale (192 cells, 5 robots), every fault mode
that matters---sending robots to explored cells, into obstacles, or into
already-covered areas---is caught faster by direct verification than by
statistical accumulation; (2)~the model runs on a Raspberry Pi Zero~2W
without significant computational overhead; and (3)~a single, explainable
detection mechanism is easier to validate, debug, and present to
reviewers than a layered system with multiple threshold types.

%---------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Impeachment}
Impeachment is triggered when a follower's trust in the leader drops
below the impeachment threshold~$\tau_{\text{imp}} = 0.3$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Impeach} = \mathbb{I}\!\bigl(T_{\text{leader}} <
    \tau_{\text{imp}}\bigr)
\end{equation}
The three-tier suspicion mechanism is the sole trust signal.  A cooldown
period prevents oscillatory leader churn.  Impeachment requires
$k_{\text{imp}} = \lceil (T_0 - \tau_{\text{imp}}) /
\Delta_{\text{decay}} \rceil = 4$ threshold crossings
(Eq.~\ref{eq:impeach_crossings}), yielding $4 \times 2 = 8$~commands
under Tier~1 evidence or $4 \times 8 = 32$~commands under Tier~3
evidence alone.

\subsection{Leader Election}
Upon impeachment, a new leader is elected by democratic vote.  Each
robot votes for the highest-trust eligible candidate from its local
trust ledger.  The eligible candidate set is restricted to agents whose
trust exceeds a voting threshold:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{E} = \bigl\{i : T_{\cdot,i} > T_{\text{thresh}}\bigr\}
    \label{eq:eligible_set}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{thresh}} = 0.5$.  The new leader is the eligible
candidate receiving the most votes:
\begin{equation}
    \text{leader}_{\text{new}} = \arg\max_{i \in \mathcal{E}} \;
    \bigl|\{j : v_j = i\}\bigr|
    \label{eq:election}
\end{equation}
where $v_j$ is robot~$j$'s vote.  Ties are broken by a two-level
comparator: (1)~fewest prior impeachments (tracked per-agent as
$\text{failures}(i)$), (2)~lowest robot~ID.  Trust is already
encoded in the vote: each robot votes for the highest-trust eligible
candidate, so aggregate trust influences the outcome through
the voting process itself.  The failure counter prevents a repeatedly
impeached agent from cycling back into leadership through tie-breaking
alone.

Upon election, trust in the new leader resets to 1.0 and suspicion
resets to 0, providing a clean evaluation window.
Non-leader peers recover trust gradually via
\begin{equation}
    T_{i,j}(t{+}1) = \min\bigl(1,\; T_{i,j}(t) + \rho\bigr),
    \quad j \neq \text{leader}
    \label{eq:trust_recovery}
\end{equation}
where $\rho = 0.005$ per broadcast cycle.  This slow recovery ensures
that a previously impeached agent can be re-elected after demonstrating
sustained good behavior, but cannot ``wash out'' accumulated distrust
from a few cycles of normality.

A \emph{fast-start} mechanism accelerates initial leader election: the
first three elections occur at 0.5\,s intervals before switching to the
normal 2.0\,s election interval.  This eliminates the startup latency
where robots sit idle waiting for the first election to converge.

\subsection{Peer Anomaly Detection}
Non-leader peers are monitored for behavioral anomalies to prevent
compromised agents from inheriting leadership:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stuck}: $< 50\,$mm movement over $\geq 2\,$s;
    \item \textbf{Spinning}: angular change $> \pi\,$rad with translation
    $< 100\,$mm over $\geq 1\,$s;
    \item \textbf{Coverage stall}: zero new cells over $\geq 5\,$s
    (evaluated only when the current leader is trusted, since a bad
    leader's commands cause all followers to stall).
\end{itemize}
Peer trust evolves as:
\begin{equation}
    T_{\text{peer}}(t{+}1) = \begin{cases}
        \max\bigl(T_{\min},\; T_{\text{peer}}(t) - \delta_a\bigr)
            & \text{if anomaly detected} \\[4pt]
        \min\bigl(1,\; T_{\text{peer}}(t) + \rho_p\bigr)
            & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:peer_trust}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_a = 0.02$ (anomaly penalty) and $\rho_p = 0.005$
(recovery rate).  The asymmetric rates ($\delta_a / \rho_p = 4$) ensure
that a single anomaly event takes four normal cycles to recover,
preventing a briefly compromised agent from immediately regaining
election eligibility.

\subsection{Frontier Assignment}
All experimental conditions use the same frontier assignment to isolate
the governance layer's contribution.  The leader uses greedy
nearest-frontier allocation~\cite{burgard2000collaborative}: robots are
sorted by distance to their nearest frontier cell (preventing systematic
ID bias), and each is assigned the nearest unassigned frontier with
\emph{assignment persistence}---a robot retains its target until the cell
is explored, preventing assignment thrashing that would cause inefficient
zigzag movement.

\subsection{Communication}
Agents communicate via UDP broadcast at 5\,Hz.  Each broadcast includes
robot state (position, heading, trust scores), vote, visited cells
(last 100), and leader assignments.  Stale assignments older than 2\,s
are automatically discarded.  The broadcast rate determines the
temporal resolution of fault detection: at 5\,Hz, the 2-command Tier~1
bound corresponds to 0.4\,s worst-case detection.

\section{Multiroom Experiments}
\label{sec:hw_sim}
%=========================================================================

To rigorously validate the proactive trust model at statistical scale, we
conduct $N{=}100$ experiments per condition using a physics-based
simulation that is functionally identical to the hardware deployment.  Robot processes run
the full REIP/Raft node software, communicate via UDP, and operate in a
multiroom arena with wall collisions---the only difference from hardware
is the localization source (simulated positions vs.\ overhead camera).

\subsection{Protocol}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Arena}: $2000 \times 1500\,$mm multiroom layout
    (Fig.~\ref{fig:arena}) with a vertical interior wall
    ($x{=}1000$, $y{=}0$--$1200\,$mm) creating a passage at the top.
    $16 \times 12 = 192$ cells of $125\,$mm.
    \item \textbf{Robots}: 5 per trial, seed-deterministic starting
    positions.
    \item \textbf{Duration}: 120\,s per trial.
    \item \textbf{Trials}: $N{=}100$ per condition, seeds 1000--100000
    (step 1000), shared across all conditions for paired comparison.
    \item \textbf{Physics}: Time-based movement at ${\sim}500\,$mm/s with
    wall-collision detection, consistent regardless of CPU load.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Controllers}
We evaluate the proposed method against two reference baselines chosen
to isolate leadership-fault resilience.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{REIP (Proposed)}: Three-tier proactive trust, merit-based
    election, democratic impeachment, governed fallback.
    \item \textbf{Raft Consensus (Baseline)}~\cite{ongaro2014raft}:
    Heartbeat-based leader election; followers obey commands without trust
    verification.  Raft is \emph{not} a Byzantine-fault-tolerant protocol:
    it detects crashed leaders via heartbeat timeout but has no mechanism
    for detecting a live leader that issues degraded commands.
    \item \textbf{Decentralized (Reference)}: No leader; each robot
    independently explores using broadcast deconfliction with the same
    planner.  Because no leader exists, leadership faults are
    inapplicable---this baseline establishes an upper bound on
    fault-immune but uncoordinated performance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Fault Conditions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Clean (NoFault)}: No faults injected.  Establishes
    baseline performance and confirms that the trust model does not
    degrade efficiency when the leader is reliable.
    \item \textbf{Bad Leader (Byzantine Assignment)}: At $t{=}10\,$s,
    Robot~1 is compromised to persistently assign already-explored cells
    as targets.  Followers are directed toward cells that provide zero
    new coverage.  At $t{=}30\,$s, a second bad-leader fault targets the
    \emph{current} leader (typically a different robot, since Robot~1 was
    already impeached by REIP), testing repeated-attack survival.  This
    directly exercises Tier~1 detection: followers recognize that the
    assigned cell is one they have personally visited.
    \item \textbf{Freeze Leader (Byzantine Stale Assignment)}: At
    $t{=}10\,$s, Robot~1 stops updating frontier assignments, caching
    and re-broadcasting the last valid assignment indefinitely.
    Followers are locked to targets that were relevant when the fault
    began but become stale as the environment is explored.  At
    $t{=}30\,$s, a second freeze fault targets the current leader.
    Unlike Byzantine Assignment, the initial commands may appear
    reasonable---the assignments were valid when issued---so detection
    relies on Tier~1 accumulation as cached targets transition from
    unexplored to explored over time.
\end{itemize}

Both fault conditions target \emph{leadership} specifically.  This is a
deliberate design choice: REIP's core contribution is trust-based
governance for leader--follower systems.  Leadership faults produce zero
coverage impact on Decentralized (which has no leader) and catastrophic
impact on Raft (which has no trust model).  The dual sequential injection
tests whether REIP can survive \emph{two} consecutive Byzantine
compromises in a single trial---and whether Raft can survive even one.

\subsubsection{Resilience Gap}
We define the \emph{resilience gap} $\Delta_R$ as the signed difference
between clean and faulty coverage for a given controller:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_R = C_{\text{fault}} - C_{\text{clean}}
    \label{eq:resilience_gap}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\text{fault}}$ and $C_{\text{clean}}$ are final coverage under
fault and clean conditions, respectively.  A perfectly resilient system
yields $\Delta_R = 0$; large negative $\Delta_R$ indicates the controller
cannot recover from the fault.

causing coverage to climb briefly.  Once followers receive the next
round of assignments, they are permanently redirected to
already-explored cells.  Coverage flatlines and never recovers.

REIP's curve shows a brief inflection around $t{=}10\,$s as followers
detect the fault, followed by resumed climbing after impeachment at
${\sim}t{=}12\,$s.  The second fault at $t{=}30\,$s produces an even
smaller inflection---the system has already demonstrated it can
recover, and does so again within seconds.

\subsubsection{Cumulative Coverage Efficiency}
To capture both convergence speed and final coverage in a single metric,
we compute the \emph{cumulative coverage efficiency} $\eta$: the area
under the coverage-vs-time curve normalized by trial duration,
\begin{equation}
    \eta = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^{T} C(t)\, dt
    \label{eq:efficiency}
\end{equation}
where $C(t) \in [0,1]$ is fractional coverage and $T = 120\,$s.  Higher
$\eta$ means faster, more sustained coverage.

Table~\ref{tab:mr_efficiency} shows $\eta$ across conditions.  REIP's
efficiency is effectively \emph{invariant} to the bad-leader fault:
$\eta = 74.8\%$ under attack vs.\ $76.7\%$ clean ($-1.9\,$pp).  This
near-zero difference confirms that fast impeachment absorbs the fault
before it can meaningfully reduce integrated coverage.  In contrast,
Raft loses $11.2\,$pp ($70.6\% \to 59.4\%$) because followers spend the
majority of the trial chasing explored cells after fault injection.

\subsubsection{Dual Sequential Fault Survival}
The second fault injection at $t{=}30\,$s targets whoever is currently
the leader---typically a \emph{different} robot from the first fault
target, since Robot~1 was already impeached.  REIP successfully
impeaches the second compromised leader in 97 of 100 bad-leader trials
and 99 of 100 freeze-leader trials, demonstrating that the trust model
handles repeated Byzantine attacks across different fault mechanisms,
not just single-shot recovery.  For Raft, the second injection is a
no-op: the system is already permanently compromised.

\subsubsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}
To isolate the contribution of each REIP component, we ran an ablation
study ($N{=}30$ per variant) under the bad-leader fault.  Three
variants were tested alongside REIP-Full:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No~Trust}: trust assessment and impeachment disabled;
    elections still occur but there is no detection mechanism.
    \item \textbf{No~Direction}: direction consistency check disabled;
    detection relies solely on the three-tier cell-level verification.
    \item \textbf{No~Causality}: causality grace period $\delta_b = 0$;
    any cell appearing in the visited set triggers immediate suspicion
    regardless of when it was explored.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{No~Trust} collapses to $39.7\%$ mean coverage with $0\%$

No~Trust has zero detection mechanism.  This is the single

offers no resilience advantage.  Trust is not an optional enhancement;
it is the entire mechanism.

\textbf{No~Direction} achieves $86.0\%$ coverage, a $4.9\,$pp penalty

faults ($97\%$ detection rate), but the direction consistency check
provides supplementary evidence that accelerates suspicion accumulation,
explaining the coverage difference.

is operationally unusable despite high fault-condition coverage.

\emph{deployable}: it suppresses false positives from concurrent
exploration without meaningfully slowing detection of genuine faults.

\subsection{Experimental Design}

Hardware experiments compare three configurations under identical arena
layout and starting positions using the same seeds as the multiroom
simulation (Section~\ref{sec:hw_sim}) for direct sim-to-real comparison:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{REIP-Full (Proposed)}: three-tier trust, election,
    impeachment, governed fallback;
    \item \textbf{Decentralized (Reference)}: no leader; each robot
    independently explores with broadcast deconfliction using the same
    planner;
    \item \textbf{Raft Consensus (Baseline)}: leader replaced only on
    heartbeat timeout; no trust or performance
    reasoning~\cite{ongaro2014raft}.
\end{itemize}

These baselines are chosen to isolate leadership-fault resilience, not
as universal comparators.  The decentralized baseline is structurally
immune to leadership faults (it has no leader); it establishes whether
coordination provides value over independence.  The Raft-style baseline
represents the standard distributed-systems approach to leader
failure---it is \emph{not} a Byzantine fault-tolerant (BFT) protocol,
so poor performance under Byzantine faults is expected.  The comparison
quantifies precisely \emph{how much} trust-based governance gains over
heartbeat-only detection.  All configurations share the same frontier
planner and motor controller.

Faults are injected via UDP:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Motor faults} (spin, stop, erratic): caught by peer
    anomaly detection;
    \item \textbf{Byzantine Assignment} (\texttt{bad\_leader}): leader
    assigns already-explored cells, directly triggering the three-tier
    trust model;
    \item \textbf{Freeze Leader} (\texttt{freeze\_leader}): leader stops
    updating frontier assignments, re-broadcasting stale cached targets
    that become increasingly wasteful as the environment is explored.
\end{itemize}

The two leadership faults are the primary demonstrations.  Byzantine
Assignment triggers immediate Tier~1 violations (personal visit ground
truth), while Freeze Leader triggers delayed Tier~1 violations as cached
targets transition from unexplored to explored over time.
Both accumulate suspicion through the three-tier model and trigger
impeachment within seconds.

\subsection{Dual-Vector Visualization}
\label{sec:dualvec}
Each robot broadcasts both its leader-commanded target and its locally
predicted optimal target.  Post-processing overlays these as colored
vectors on overhead footage:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Green arrow}: robot's predicted movement direction
    (from its own low-resolution belief map);
    \item \textbf{Red arrow}: leader's commanded direction (the
    assignment the robot was told to follow).
\end{itemize}

When these vectors are aligned, the leader is issuing commands consistent
with each robot's local knowledge.  When they diverge---the robot's
prediction points toward unexplored territory while the leader's command
points toward already-explored cells---the visual evidence of a
compromised leader is immediately apparent.

Trust state is encoded by a halo contour around each robot:
\emph{none} (trust healthy), \emph{yellow} (suspicion accumulating),
\emph{red} (trust critical, impeachment imminent).  This visualization
provides an intuitive demonstration of REIP's detection mechanism in the
supplementary gallery (5-panel REIP sequence vs.\ 5-panel Raft

\subsubsection{Clean Performance}
Under clean conditions (no faults), REIP-Full and Raft-style baselines
are expected to achieve comparable coverage rates, as the governance
layer acts as a passive monitor.  The decentralized baseline is expected
to show lower coverage efficiency due to redundant exploration without
coordinated assignment.

\subsubsection{Fault Resilience}
Under the \texttt{bad\_leader} fault, the key differentiator is
detection mechanism.  REIP detects the fault proactively through
three-tier command verification: Tier~1 evidence (personal visit) yields
detection within 2~commands ($< 1\,$s at 5\,Hz broadcast rate).
Raft-style failover cannot detect a misbehaving leader that maintains
heartbeats, and decentralized mode is unaffected by leadership faults
but sacrifices coordination efficiency.

\subsubsection{Detection Timing}
The theoretical bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:detection_bound}) predicts
$n_{\text{detect}} = 2$ commands under Tier~1 evidence.  Simulation
results (median $0.21\,$s first suspicion) validate this bound.
Hardware detection times are expected to show modest additional latency
from real sensor noise and WiFi broadcast jitter.

\section{Discussion}
%=========================================================================

\subsection{Why Proactive Detection Matters}
\label{sec:discussion_evolution}
The proactive model provides a provable worst-case detection bound
(Eq.~\ref{eq:detection_bound}) that reactive approaches cannot match.
Reactive detectors require $O(1/\epsilon^2)$ observations to distinguish
a faulty leader from noise at confidence level $\epsilon$, because they
operate on statistical residuals accumulated \emph{after} commands are
executed.  The proactive model's bound is $\lceil \sigma / (w_{\min} -
r) \rceil = 8$ commands under worst-case Tier~3 evidence, tightening to
2~commands under Tier~1 ground-truth evidence.  This is a structural
advantage: detection speed scales with evidence quality, not sample size.

The three-tier confidence weighting reflects an epistemological hierarchy
in local knowledge.  A cell the robot physically visited is ground truth
regardless of distance or time.  A ToF reading is verifiable within
sensor range.  Peer-reported coverage may be stale.  Weighting evidence
by source reliability prevents both false positives (impeaching a good
leader from stale peer data) and false negatives (ignoring verifiable bad
commands).  The causality-aware timestamp check further suppresses false
positives from concurrent exploration without weakening genuine fault
detection.

The cumulative coverage efficiency $\eta$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:efficiency})
quantifies the practical consequence: REIP's $\eta$ is nearly identical
under bad-leader attack and clean conditions ($-1.9\,$pp),
meaning the total integrated coverage loss during fault detection and
recovery is negligible.  The governance layer absorbs faults fast enough
that the coverage trajectory barely deflects.

\subsection{Resilience as Invariance}
The most compelling result from the multiroom experiments is not that
REIP beats Raft under fault conditions---it is that REIP's performance
is \emph{nearly invariant} to fault conditions.  The median coverage gap
between REIP-clean and both fault conditions is $0.0$~percentage points
(Table~\ref{tab:mr_resilience}).  The mean gap is only $-0.3\,$pp under
bad-leader and actually \emph{positive} ($+4.9\,$pp) under
freeze-leader.  Faults trigger detection, impeachment, and recovery fast
enough that the coverage trajectory barely deflects
(Fig.~\ref{fig:resilience_contrast}).

This invariance holds across two qualitatively different fault types:
immediate misbehavior (bad-leader) and gradual staleness
(freeze-leader).  REIP's exploration performance does not meaningfully
depend on whether the leader is compromised or on the fault mechanism.
The governance layer absorbs the fault and the mission continues.

\subsection{Why Leadership Matters}
A natural objection is that decentralized exploration avoids the
single-point-of-failure problem entirely.  However, leadership provides
two advantages that decentralized systems cannot replicate.

\textbf{Coordination quality.}  A leader with a merged global map
assigns non-overlapping frontiers, prevents spatial clustering, and
maximizes information gain per robot per timestep.  Decentralized agents
operating on partial information inevitably duplicate coverage---each
robot independently selects its nearest frontier, often the same frontier
chosen by neighbors.

\textbf{Computational division of labor.}  The leader absorbs the most
expensive operations---global map fusion, frontier scoring, and
coordinated task allocation---while followers perform only lightweight
local tasks: merging peer maps, executing waypoints, and evaluating
trust.  This structured information flow (followers report $\to$ leader
computes $\to$ leader broadcasts) is particularly valuable on
resource-constrained platforms.  In our hardware testbed, each robot runs
on a Raspberry Pi Zero~2W with limited CPU and memory; centralizing
allocation on a single elected node keeps per-agent compute tractable
without sacrificing team-level optimality.

The multiroom results (Section~\ref{sec:hw_sim}) quantify this
tradeoff.  Under clean conditions, REIP and decentralized achieve comparable
catastrophic failure rates ($11/100$ vs.\ $13/100$) despite the overhead
of leadership election.  Under adversarial faults, Raft cannot detect a
misbehaving leader that maintains heartbeats, while decentralized mode
sacrifices coordination entirely.  REIP preserves \emph{both}
advantages---coordination quality and computational efficiency---through
rapid, principled detection and recovery: it does not replace
coordination with autonomy; it ensures that coordination \emph{survives}
compromised leadership.

\subsection{Governed Fallback and Ablation Insights}
Prior ablation in the GridWorld (2\,000 trials, supplemental data)
confirmed that governance and fallback form a single mechanism.
The multiroom ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) deepens this
finding by isolating \emph{within} governance: the No~Trust variant
collapses to $39.7\%$ coverage ($0\%$ detection), demonstrating that
trust assessment is the primary contributor.  No~Causality achieves higher
fault-condition coverage ($96.1\%$) but at $8.1$ false positives per
clean trial---operationally unusable.  The combined system uses
trust as the switching signal: follow when trusted, fall back when
trust drops, resume following after a new leader is elected.  Causality
gating is what makes this signal reliable.

\subsection{Limitations}
This study is limited to 2D environments, frontier-based exploration,
and leader-centric fault models.  Hardware validation uses a small arena
with five homogeneous robots.  The three-tier confidence weights are
calibrated for this scale; larger deployments with higher broadcast
latency may require adjusted peer weights.  Simulation fault profiles are
predetermined rather than live LLM-generated.  Parameters are fixed
rather than adaptively tuned.

Some seeds produce catastrophic trials for all controllers ($11/100$ for
REIP under bad-leader), driven by initial position configurations that
cause robots to wedge against walls.  These failures are not
governance-related but reflect limitations in the low-level path planner.

We do not prove that REIP converges to a stable leader in all cases.
Under pathological conditions (e.g., all agents equally faulty), the
election mechanism may cycle.  We mitigate this empirically with
cooldown periods and the leader-failure counter in tie-breaking, and
note that the worst-case detection bound
(Eq.~\ref{eq:detection_bound}) holds regardless of convergence---each
follower's detection is independent and bounded.

\subsection{Reproducibility}
All raw data, deterministic seeds, experiment scripts, and \LaTeX{}
source are provided in a supplemental repository.  Every figure and
table in this paper can be regenerated from the provided seeds and
harness scripts.  The repository is available on USB at presentation.

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
Several extensions are planned.  \textbf{Command pattern analysis}:
monitoring temporal patterns in commanded directions (e.g., angular
rate, entropy of target sequences) could detect collectively harmful
command sequences that pass individual verification; learned models
trained on command histories could generalize beyond pre-specified fault
types.
\textbf{Map poisoning}: compromised robots broadcasting false visited
cells could corrupt the shared belief map---REIP does not currently
defend against decentralized data-plane attacks.  \textbf{Tier~2
validation}: hardware deployment with physical ToF sensors would enable
testing whether the leader can direct followers into walls.
\textbf{Scale}: larger arenas, more robots, and 3D environments where
adaptive confidence weights based on observed broadcast reliability
become necessary.  \textbf{Adaptive weights}: learning tier weights
from deployment data could improve detection sensitivity for specific
environments.

\section{Conclusion}
%=========================================================================

This paper introduced REIP, a trust-based governance framework that
enables multi-robot teams to detect, impeach, and replace compromised
leaders without centralized oversight or ground-truth oracle access.

The core mechanism is a proactive three-tier trust model that verifies
leader commands \emph{before execution} against local knowledge ranked
by source reliability.  This yields a closed-form worst-case detection
bound of $\lceil \sigma / (w_{\min} - r) \rceil$ commands---2~under
ground-truth evidence, 8~under peer reports---enabling sub-second fault
detection without wasting physical movement on bad commands.

In multiroom experiments ($N{=}100$ per condition), REIP achieves:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $100\%$ median coverage under both clean and adversarial
    conditions---fault-invariant performance;
    \item Median fault detection in $0.20\,$s, impeachment in
    $1.21\,$s (bad-leader) and $1.67\,$s (freeze-leader), with
    $97{-}99\%$ detection rate;
    \item $+24.5\,$pp mean coverage advantage over Raft under Byzantine
    Assignment ($90.9\%$ vs.\ $66.4\%$) and $+32.2\,$pp under Freeze
    Leader ($96.1\%$ vs.\ $63.9\%$);
    \item Survival of two sequential Byzantine compromises in a single
    trial;
    \item Ablation confirmation that removing the trust model collapses
    coverage to $39.7\%$, confirming it is the primary mechanism.
\end{itemize}

REIP addresses a failure mode that existing approaches cannot: a leader
that is alive, communicating, and issuing degraded commands.  Raft
detects only crash failures via heartbeat timeout.  Decentralized
exploration avoids the problem by abandoning coordination entirely.
REIP preserves the coordination advantage of hierarchical leadership
while making that advantage resilient to arbitrary leadership faults
through rapid, principled detection and democratic recovery.
