\begin{abstract}
%=========================================================================
Multi-agent robotic teams are increasingly deployed for exploration, mapping, and coverage tasks where coordinated action outperforms individual platforms. Within these teams, leader-follower architectures offer efficient coordination through centralized task allocation, but introduce a critical vulnerability: if the leader becomes compromised and issues faulty commands, mission quality degrades rapidly. This is especially dangerous in time-critical missions such as avalanche search-and-rescue, where delays waste coverage and endanger lives. This study investigated whether a robot team could detect and remove a faulty leader without centralized supervision or access to a ground-truth map.
The study proposes the Resilient Election and Impeachment Policy (REIP), a trust-based governance framework in which follower robots verify leader commands before execution using three levels of local evidence: personal visit history, sensor readings, and peer-reported coverage. When commands conflict with local evidence, suspicion accumulates through a trust ledger. If trust falls below a threshold, followers vote to impeach the leader and elect a replacement. REIP was evaluated in a physics-based simulation running 100 trials per condition across three controllers and multiple fault types, then validated on a five-robot embedded hardware platform (\$15 per compute board) under real-world sensing and communication noise.
Under both normal and faulty-leader conditions, REIP maintained full median coverage, detecting faults within fractions of a second and completing leader replacement within two seconds. Under faulty-leader conditions, REIP outperformed a heartbeat-only baseline by over 24\% in mean coverage, and an ablation study confirmed that removing the trust model collapsed coverage below 40\%. These results demonstrate that proactive trust-based governance makes coordinated robot teams resilient to compromised leadership in time-critical missions.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%=========================================================================

\subsection{Motivation and Context}

Multi-robot systems are increasingly used for exploration, mapping, and coverage tasks in domains ranging from disaster response and underground inspection to planetary exploration~\cite{queralta2020sar, murphy2004rescue, chung2023darpa}. Among these, time-critical missions impose the strictest demands on coverage speed and reliability: avalanche and wilderness search-and-rescue survival decreases sharply as rescue is delayed, making fast and complete area coverage essential~\cite{rauch2024avalanche}.

Coordinating a team of robots requires choosing how decisions are made. In fully \emph{centralized} architectures a single planner directs all agents, offering global optimality but creating a bottleneck and single point of failure. In fully \emph{decentralized} systems each agent acts on local information and reaches agreement through consensus---a process by which distributed agents converge on shared decisions without a central authority---yielding robustness at the cost of coordination efficiency. \emph{Leader-follower} architectures sit between these extremes: a designated leader allocates search zones and maintains a global picture of progress~\cite{yamauchi1997frontier, burgard2000collaborative, simmons2000coordination}, while followers retain local autonomy to execute assigned tasks. This structure combines the coordination benefit of centralization with the scalability of decentralized execution.

However, leader-follower coordination introduces a critical vulnerability: if the leader becomes compromised or faulty, the rest of the team continues executing poor commands, wasting time, energy, and coverage. In missions such as avalanche search-and-rescue, even minutes of unnecessary delay can determine survival outcomes~\cite{rauch2024avalanche}.

Beyond search-and-rescue, the same vulnerability appears wherever autonomous teams operate without human oversight. Autonomous mining fleets require resilient coordination when individual vehicles degrade~\cite{marshall2016robotics}. The DARPA Subterranean Challenge confirmed that coordinated multi-robot teams consistently outperform single platforms in underground mapping~\cite{chung2023darpa}, and NASA's CADRE mission will deploy cooperative autonomous rovers on the lunar surface where communication blackouts make centralized human control infeasible~\cite{delacroix2024cadre, whittaker2012lunar}. On Mars, one-way communication delays of 4--24~minutes eliminate human-in-the-loop oversight entirely; robot teams must self-govern~\cite{mcbrayer2022comm}.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

This vulnerability can manifest through sensor degradation, software faults, erroneous outputs from AI-based decision systems~\cite{ji2023hallucination}, or deliberate interference~\cite{douceur2002sybil}. Critically, a compromised leader does not need to crash to cause mission failure; it only needs to remain active while issuing low-quality or misleading assignments.

In distributed systems, \emph{Byzantine faults} refer to arbitrary failures where a node may behave in any manner---including sending contradictory or harmful messages---as opposed to simple crash failures where a node stops responding. Classical Byzantine fault-tolerant (BFT) protocols provide guarantees against such faults but assume reliable all-to-all communication and honest supermajorities, assumptions that are often unrealistic in range-limited robotic teams with intermittent connectivity~\cite{lamport1982byzantine, castro1999practical}. Raft, a widely used consensus protocol for crash-tolerant distributed systems, detects leader failure through periodic liveness signals called heartbeats~\cite{ongaro2014raft}. However, a leader that continues transmitting heartbeats while issuing degraded commands remains invisible to Raft. In parallel, frontier-based exploration methods typically assume that agents act in good faith~\cite{yamauchi1997frontier, burgard2000collaborative}, and existing computational trust frameworks are rarely coupled with explicit impeachment-and-replacement mechanisms for robotic teams~\cite{marsh1994formalising}.

In search-and-rescue missions, avoidable time delay and error is unacceptable. The core problem, therefore, is how a robot team can detect and remove a leader that is still alive and communicating, but no longer making trustworthy decisions, before mission-critical damage occurs.

\subsection{Proposed Solution}

This paper proposes the Resilient Election and Impeachment Policy (REIP), a trust-based governance framework for multi-robot teams. REIP enables followers to detect compromised leaders, impeach them through distributed voting, and elect replacements without centralized oversight or access to a ground-truth oracle.

The core mechanism is a proactive trust model in which each robot verifies leader commands before execution against its own local knowledge. REIP uses three-tier confidence-weighted suspicion accumulation, ranking evidence by source reliability: personal experience outweighs sensor readings, which outweigh peer reports. This allows the system to respond quickly to high-confidence faults while reducing false positives caused by stale or indirect information. Existing reactive fault detection approaches---including CUSUM and Bayesian anomaly detectors~\cite{page1954cusum, gelman2013bayesian, chandola2009anomaly}---identify faulty behavior only after damage has already propagated through the system, introducing detection latency that wastes physical time, battery, and coverage. REIP's proactive model avoids this cost by catching suspect commands before they are executed, making it both faster and more efficient under the sparse, lossy communication conditions typical of real-world robotic deployments.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item A trust-based governance framework for multi-robot teams that enables distributed leader fault detection, democratic impeachment, and merit-based leader replacement without centralized oversight or ground-truth oracle access.
    \item A proactive three-tier confidence-weighted trust model that verifies leader commands before execution using local knowledge ranked by source reliability, together with a closed-form worst-case detection bound.
    \item A proactive detection architecture designed for sparse, lossy, and intermittent communication, with causality-aware verification that suppresses false positives from concurrent exploration by comparing visited-cell timestamps against leader assignment times.
    \item A rigorous simulation evaluation with $N{=}100$ trials per condition, comparing REIP against Raft and decentralized baselines under dual sequential Byzantine fault injection, plus an ablation study quantifying the role of each major component.
    \item Hardware validation on a five-robot embedded platform (\$15 Raspberry Pi Zero~2W, 512\,MB RAM per robot; \$514 total system cost) with overhead ArUco-based localization~\cite{garrido2014aruco}, demonstrating that Byzantine-resilient governance is achievable on ultra-low-cost hardware.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
%=========================================================================
REIP lies at the intersection of Byzantine fault tolerance, multi-robot frontier exploration, and trust-based coordination in multi-agent systems. We briefly review each area and highlight the gap that motivates our approach.

\subsection{Byzantine Fault Tolerance and Distributed Governance}

Classical BFT protocols provide strong guarantees against arbitrary faults under assumptions of reliable communication and honest supermajorities~\cite{lamport1982byzantine}. Practical protocols such as PBFT relax some synchrony assumptions~\cite{castro1999practical}, and the widely used Raft protocol provides leader election and log replication for crash-tolerant distributed systems~\cite{ongaro2014raft}. However, these assumptions are often difficult to satisfy in range-limited robot teams.

REIP adopts the spirit of BFT --explicitly modeling faulty leaders and governing their authority --but operates under partial observability and lossy communication. Unlike Raft, which detects leader failure only via heartbeat timeout~\cite{ongaro2014raft}, REIP incorporates task-level performance reasoning into its leader assessment. This distinction is critical: a leader that is alive and communicating but issuing degraded commands (e.g., from sensor hallucination or adversarial compromise) will never trigger Raft's timeout-based detection, leaving the team under indefinite faulty leadership.

\subsection{Multi-Robot Frontier Exploration}

Frontier-based exploration assigns robots to boundaries between known and unknown space~\cite{yamauchi1997frontier}, with multi-robot extensions coordinating assignments to maximize coverage rate~\cite{burgard2000collaborative}. Leader--follower architectures exploit a global view for coordinated assignment~\cite{simmons2000coordination}, but treat the leader as implicitly trustworthy. There is no mechanism for detecting when the leader's directions become counterproductive, nor for replacing the leader without external supervision. REIP augments a standard frontier-based leader--follower stack with a governance layer that monitors, impeaches, and replaces a failing leader based on observed performance---giving each agent the autonomy to question orders and, collectively, to act on that judgment.

\subsection{Trust and Reputation in Multi-Agent Systems}

Computational trust frameworks quantify agent reliability from observed behavior~\cite{marsh1994formalising}, and have been applied in peer-to-peer networks, e-commerce, and multi-agent simulations~\cite{ramchurn2004trust}. However, most trust models assume access to ground-truth outcomes: a buyer can verify whether the product was delivered correctly. In multi-robot exploration under partial observability, ground truth is not directly available; agents observe only local coverage changes and peer broadcasts. This gap motivates a belief-only trust approach that operates without an oracle map --- the design described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}. Rather than measuring trust from outcomes---which requires following potentially bad commands and only reacting after damage has occurred---the proactive model verifies leader commands against local knowledge before execution. This eliminates the detection latency inherent in reactive approaches and makes the framework robust under the sparse, lossy communication conditions typical of embedded robotic platforms.

\section{REIP Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%=========================================================================

REIP operates as a governance layer atop a frontier-based leader--follower exploration stack. Each robot maintains a high-resolution local map of personally observed cells, a low-resolution global belief map aggregated from peer broadcasts, and a coverage/motion history. Robots share compressed belief updates and state via peer-to-peer broadcast. The leader assigns frontiers using a common exploration policy. Followers verify those assignments against local evidence before execution. When trust falls below threshold, the team impeaches the leader and elects a replacement. The following subsections detail the verification pipeline, detection mechanics, and governance procedures. Table~\ref{tab:notation} consolidates the notation used throughout this section. Fig.~\ref{fig:flowchart} shows the entire process of processing commands from start to the trust update.

\subsection{Information Zones}
\label{sec:info_zones}

Each robot maintains two overlapping information zones that determine what it can verify about a leader's command:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-resolution zone}: cells the robot has personally visited. This is absolute ground truth regardless of the robot's current position or distance from those cells---the memory of having been there is permanent.
    \item \textbf{Local zone}: cells currently within time-of-flight (ToF) sensor range ($200\,$mm). Obstacle readings here are instantaneous physical measurements that are regenerated each cycle and do not accumulate.
\end{itemize}

Beyond these zones, the robot relies on the low-resolution global belief map built from peer broadcasts. This data may be stale: a cell reported as unexplored 2 seconds ago may have been visited by another robot since the last broadcast. The three-tier model weights evidence accordingly.

\subsection{Three-Tier Evidence Verification}
\label{sec:hw_trust}

When the leader assigns a target cell, the follower checks it against three tiers of local knowledge. We formalize the ordering as follows.

\begin{definition}[Evidence Ordering]
\label{def:evidence}
Evidence sources are totally ordered by reliability:
\[
    \text{Tier\,1\,(personal)} \;\succ\;
    \text{Tier\,2\,(sensor)} \;\succ\;
    \text{Tier\,3\,(peer)},
\]
with confidence weights $w_1 = w_2 = 1.0 > w_3 = 0.3$.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Tier~1}: the assigned cell is in $\mathcal{V}_i$; the ground-truth. Harmful commands are more obvious at this tier.
    \item \textbf{Tier~2}: the assigned cell is within ToF range and contains a detected obstacle (sensor validation). This also prevents forced collision between peer follower robots.
    \item \textbf{Tier~3}: the assigned cell has been reported as explored by another robot's broadcast and map merge, but robot~$i$ has not visited it personally (the report may be stale due to broadcast delay).
\end{itemize}
Higher-confidence evidence yields a tighter detection bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:detection}).
\end{definition}

Let $w_t \defeq w(c_t) + w_d(\alpha_t,\, w(c_t))$ denote the total evidence weight at cycle~$t$, including the direction-consistency bonus (Section~\ref{sec:direction_consistency}).

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Three-Tier Evidence Weighting}
\label{alg:evidence}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Target cell $c$, assignment time $t_a$
\ENSURE Evidence weight $w$
\STATE $w \leftarrow 0$
\IF{$c \in \texttt{my\_visited}$ \AND $t_{\text{visit}}(c) < t_a - \delta_b$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 1.0$
    \COMMENT{Tier~1: personal ground truth}
\ELSIF{$c$ within ToF range \AND obstacle at $c$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 1.0$
    \COMMENT{Tier~2: sensor verification}
\ELSIF{$c \in \texttt{known\_visited} \setminus \texttt{my\_visited}$
       \AND $t_{\text{known}}(c) < t_a - \delta_b$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 0.3$
    \COMMENT{Tier~3: peer-reported}
\ENDIF
\RETURN $w$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Trust Dynamics}
\label{sec:trust_dynamics}

\begin{definition}[Trust State Machine]
\label{def:trust_sm}
The per-follower trust state $(S_t, T_t)\in\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\times[T_{\min},1]$ evolves under the transition
\begin{align}
S_{t+1} &\;=\; \clip{\,S_t + w_t - r\,\Ind[w_t=0]\,},
\label{eq:suspicion_compact}\\
k_{t+1} &\;\defeq\; \left\lfloor S_{t+1}\,/\,\sigma \right\rfloor,
\label{eq:crossings}\\
T_{t+1} &\;=\; \max\!\bigl(T_{\min},\;
         T_t - k_{t+1}\,\Delta_{\text{decay}}\bigr),
\label{eq:trust_compact}\\
S_{t+1} &\;\leftarrow\; S_{t+1} - k_{t+1}\,\sigma.
\label{eq:residual}
\end{align}
Impeachment triggers when $T_{t+1} < \tau_{\text{imp}}$.
\end{definition}

In words: each time a leader command fails verification, the evidence weight is added to a running suspicion accumulator~$S_t$. When no fault evidence is present, suspicion decays at rate~$r$, allowing the system to forgive transient anomalies. Each time accumulated suspicion crosses the threshold~$\sigma$, trust drops by a fixed decrement~$\Delta_{\text{decay}}$ and the residual suspicion carries over. This creates a time-varying trust decay where persistent faults erode trust rapidly---Tier~1 evidence ($w_1{=}1.0$) crosses the threshold in just 2~commands, while weaker peer evidence ($w_3{=}0.3$) requires 8---but isolated false alarms decay away before triggering action. The parameters $\sigma$, $r$, and $\Delta_{\text{decay}}$ are fully tunable: tightening~$\sigma$ or increasing~$\Delta_{\text{decay}}$ accelerates detection at the cost of more false positives, providing a direct knob for mission-specific risk tolerance. At the default settings, full impeachment completes in a median of $1.21{-}1.67\,$s (Section~\ref{sec:results})---fast enough to limit coverage loss to under $2\%$.

The state machine is a deterministic recurrence: given the evidence stream $(w_0, w_1, \ldots)$, the trajectory of $(S_t, T_t)$ is uniquely determined. This enables the closed-form detection bound derived in Theorem~\ref{thm:detection}.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Suspicion Update and Trust Decay}
\label{alg:suspicion}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Total weight $w_{\Sigma} = w + w_d$
\IF{$w_{\Sigma} > 0$}
    \STATE $S \leftarrow S + w_{\Sigma}$
\ELSE
    \STATE $S \leftarrow \max(0,\; S - r)$
    \COMMENT{Decay toward zero}
\ENDIF
\WHILE{$S \geq \sigma$}
    \STATE $T_{\text{leader}} \leftarrow \max(T_{\min},\;
           T_{\text{leader}} - \Delta_{\text{decay}})$
    \STATE $S \leftarrow S - \sigma$
    \COMMENT{Carry-over residual}
\ENDWHILE
\IF{$T_{\text{leader}} < \tau_{\text{imp}}$}
    \STATE Trigger impeachment $\to$ Algorithm~\ref{alg:election}
\ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Detection Bound}
\label{sec:detection_bound}

The deterministic structure of Definition~\ref{def:trust_sm} yields a closed-form bound on how quickly a Byzantine leader is detected and removed.

\begin{theorem}[Worst-Case Detection Bound]
\label{thm:detection}
Under persistent adversarial commands verified with tier-$k$ evidence weight~$w_k > r$, the first trust decay occurs within
\begin{equation}
    n_{\text{detect}} = \left\lceil \frac{\sigma}{w_k - r}
    \right\rceil
    \label{eq:detection_bound}
\end{equation}
commands. Full impeachment occurs within
\begin{equation}
    n_{\text{imp}} = \underbrace{\left\lceil
    \frac{T_0 - \tau_{\text{imp}}}{\Delta_{\text{decay}}}
    \right\rceil}_{k_{\text{imp}}\text{ crossings}} \;\cdot\;
    n_{\text{detect}}
    \label{eq:impeachment_bound}
\end{equation}
commands.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Each bad command increases suspicion by at least $w_k - r > 0$ (since $w_k \geq w_3 = 0.3 > r = 0.1$ by Definition~\ref{def:evidence}). The threshold~$\sigma$ is therefore crossed after at most $\lceil\sigma/(w_k - r)\rceil$ commands; each crossing decrements trust by~$\Delta_{\text{decay}}$. The number of crossings required to reach~$\tau_{\text{imp}}$ is $k_{\text{imp}} = \lceil(T_0 - \tau_{\text{imp}})/\Delta_{\text{decay}}\rceil$; the product follows.
\end{proof}

\noindent\textbf{Instantiation.}\;
Under Tier~1 evidence ($w_1{=}1.0$, $r{=}0.1$, $\sigma{=}1.5$): $n_{\text{detect}} = \lceil 1.5/0.9 \rceil = 2$~commands. Under Tier~3 alone ($w_3{=}0.3$): $n_{\text{detect}} = \lceil 1.5/0.2 \rceil = 8$~commands. With $\Delta_{\text{decay}}{=}0.2$ and $\tau_{\text{imp}}{=}0.3$, $k_{\text{imp}} = 4$, yielding $n_{\text{imp}} = 8$ (Tier~1) or $32$ (Tier~3) commands to full impeachment. The four-fold gap between tiers directly encodes the epistemological difference between ground truth and peer hearsay.

\begin{corollary}[Liveness]
\label{cor:liveness}
If a Byzantine leader generates at least one flaggable command every $\Delta t$~seconds, impeachment occurs within $n_{\text{imp}} \cdot \Delta t$~seconds. At the 5\,Hz broadcast rate, Tier-1 evidence yields impeachment within $8 \times 0.2\,\text{s} = 1.6\,$s---consistent with the empirical median of 1.21--1.67\,s reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Causality Gating}
\label{sec:causality}

Concurrent exploration can cause false positives: a cell may be visited by a peer \emph{after} the leader computed its assignment but \emph{before} the follower verifies the command. A causality gate suppresses this by requiring that the evidence predate the assignment by at least~$\delta_b$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{flag}(c) = \mathbb{I}\bigl(c \in \texttt{known\_visited}
    \;\wedge\; t_{\text{known}}(c) < t_{\text{assign}} - \delta_b\bigr)
\end{equation}
where $\delta_b = 0.5\,$s (${\approx}\,2.5\times$ the 5\,Hz broadcast period). The gate applies to Tiers~1 and~3 (stored map evidence); Tier~2 uses instantaneous ToF data and needs no stale-data gating. A genuinely compromised leader assigns cells explored well outside this window, so detection is unaffected.

\subsection{Direction Consistency}
\label{sec:direction_consistency}

A complementary geometric check detects faults invisible to the map-based tiers---commands that point away from \emph{all} unexplored territory. The follower computes
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \bigl|\text{atan2}(\Delta y_f,
    \Delta x_f) - \text{atan2}(\Delta y_{\text{cmd}},
    \Delta x_{\text{cmd}})\bigr|,
\end{equation}
the best angular alignment between the command and any frontier cell (normalized to $[0,\pi]$). Two thresholds govern the response: $\alpha > 3\pi/4$ triggers standalone suspicion; $\pi/2 < \alpha \leq 3\pi/4$ reinforces only when the three-tier check has already flagged the command. This catches severe directional faults without penalizing legitimate distant assignments (see Algorithm~\ref{alg:direction}).

The per-command verification pipeline comprises three atomic steps: evidence weighting (Algorithm~\ref{alg:evidence}), direction consistency (Algorithm~\ref{alg:direction}), and suspicion update (Algorithm~\ref{alg:suspicion}).

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Direction Consistency Check}
\label{alg:direction}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Command direction $\theta_c$, frontier set $\mathcal{F}$,
         evidence weight $w$
\ENSURE Direction suspicion $w_d$
\STATE $\alpha \leftarrow \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}}
       \lvert\theta_f - \theta_c\rvert$
\IF{$\alpha > 3\pi/4$}
    \STATE $w_d \leftarrow 0.5 + 0.3 \cdot
           \frac{\alpha - 3\pi/4}{\pi/4}$
    \COMMENT{Severe: all frontiers behind}
\ELSIF{$\alpha > \pi/2$ \AND $w > 0$}
    \STATE $w_d \leftarrow 0.3 \cdot
           \frac{\alpha - \pi/2}{\pi/4}$
    \COMMENT{Reinforcing only}
\ELSE
    \STATE $w_d \leftarrow 0$
\ENDIF
\RETURN $w_d$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Impeachment}
\label{sec:impeachment}

The preceding checks---three-tier evidence verification (Algorithm~\ref{alg:evidence}), direction consistency (Algorithm~\ref{alg:direction}), and causality gating---each contribute a weight to the suspicion accumulator. That accumulated suspicion drives trust downward through the decay model (Algorithm~\ref{alg:suspicion}): each threshold crossing reduces trust by $\Delta_{\text{decay}}$, and when trust drops below the impeachment threshold $\tau_{\text{imp}}$, the follower triggers a vote to remove the current leader.

Formally, impeachment fires when $T_{\text{leader}} < \tau_{\text{imp}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:trust_sm}). By Theorem~\ref{thm:detection}, this requires at most $n_{\text{imp}}$ adversarial commands---8 under Tier-1 evidence, 32 under Tier-3 alone. A cooldown period after each election prevents oscillatory leader churn.

\subsection{Leader Election}
\label{sec:election}

Upon impeachment, a new leader is elected by democratic vote. Each robot votes for the highest-trust eligible candidate from its local trust ledger. The eligible candidate set is restricted to agents whose trust exceeds a voting threshold:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{E} = \bigl\{i : T_{\cdot,i} > T_{\text{thresh}}\bigr\}
    \label{eq:eligible_set}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{thresh}} = 0.5$. The new leader is the eligible candidate receiving the most votes:
\begin{equation}
    \text{leader}_{\text{new}} = \arg\max_{i \in \mathcal{E}} \;
    \bigl|\{j : v_j = i\}\bigr|
    \label{eq:election}
\end{equation}
where $v_j$ is robot~$j$'s vote. Ties are broken by a two-level comparator: (1)~fewest prior impeachments (tracked per-agent as $\text{failures}(i)$), (2)~lowest robot~ID. Trust is already encoded in the vote: each robot votes for the highest-trust eligible candidate, so aggregate trust influences the outcome through the voting process itself. The failure counter prevents a repeatedly impeached agent from cycling back into leadership through tie-breaking alone.

Upon election, trust in the new leader resets to 1.0 and suspicion resets to 0, providing a clean evaluation window. Non-leader peers recover trust gradually via
\begin{equation}
    T_{i,j}(t{+}1) = \min\bigl(1,\; T_{i,j}(t) + \rho\bigr),
    \quad j \neq \text{leader}
    \label{eq:trust_recovery}
\end{equation}
where $\rho = 0.005$ per broadcast cycle. This slow recovery ensures that a previously impeached agent can be re-elected after demonstrating sustained good behavior, but cannot erase accumulated distrust through a brief period of normal operation.

A \emph{fast-start} mechanism accelerates initial leader election: the first three elections occur at 0.5\,s intervals before switching to the normal 2.0\,s election interval. This eliminates the startup latency where robots sit idle waiting for the first election to converge.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Merit-Based Leader Election}
\label{alg:election}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Impeached leader $\ell$, trust ledger $\{T_{i,j}\}$
\ENSURE New leader $\ell_{\text{new}}$
\STATE $\mathcal{E} \leftarrow \{j : T_{\cdot,j} > T_{\text{thresh}}\}$
    \COMMENT{Eligible candidates}
\FOR{each robot $i$}
    \STATE $v_i \leftarrow \arg\max_{j \in \mathcal{E}} T_{i,j}$
    \COMMENT{Vote for highest-trust eligible}
\ENDFOR
\STATE $\text{tally}(j) \leftarrow |\{i : v_i = j\}| \;\forall\, j \in \mathcal{E}$
\STATE $\ell_{\text{new}} \leftarrow \arg\max_{j \in \mathcal{E}}
       \bigl(\text{tally}(j),\;
       {-}\text{failures}(j),\;
       {-}j\bigr)$
    \COMMENT{Votes $\to$ fewest failures $\to$ lowest ID}
\STATE $T_{\cdot,\ell_{\text{new}}} \leftarrow 1.0$;\;
       $S_{\ell_{\text{new}}} \leftarrow 0$
    \COMMENT{Clean slate}
\RETURN $\ell_{\text{new}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Peer Anomaly Detection}
\label{sec:peer_anomaly}

Non-leader peers are monitored for behavioral anomalies to prevent compromised agents from inheriting leadership:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stuck}: $< 50\,$mm movement over $\geq 2\,$s;
    \item \textbf{Spinning}: angular change $> \pi\,$rad with translation $< 100\,$mm over $\geq 1\,$s;
    \item \textbf{Coverage stall}: zero new cells over $\geq 5\,$s (evaluated only when the current leader is trusted, since a bad leader's commands cause all followers to stall).
\end{itemize}
Peer trust evolves as:
\begin{equation}
    T_{\text{peer}}(t{+}1) = \begin{cases}
        \max\bigl(T_{\min},\; T_{\text{peer}}(t) - \delta_a\bigr)
            & \text{if anomaly = true} \\[4pt]
        \min\bigl(1,\; T_{\text{peer}}(t) + \rho_p\bigr)
            & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:peer_trust}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_a = 0.02$ (anomaly penalty) and $\rho_p = 0.005$ (recovery rate). The asymmetric rates ($\delta_a / \rho_p = 4$) ensure that a single anomaly event takes four normal cycles to recover, preventing a briefly compromised agent from immediately regaining election eligibility.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Peer Anomaly Detection}
\label{alg:peer_anomaly}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\FOR{each non-leader peer $j$}
    \IF{$\lVert\Delta\mathbf{p}_j\rVert < 50\,$mm over $\geq 2\,$s}
        \STATE anomaly $\leftarrow$ \textsc{Stuck}
    \ELSIF{$|\Delta\theta_j| > \pi$ \AND
           $\lVert\Delta\mathbf{p}_j\rVert < 100\,$mm over $\geq 1\,$s}
        \STATE anomaly $\leftarrow$ \textsc{Spinning}
    \ELSIF{new cells $= 0$ over $\geq 5\,$s \AND leader trusted}
        \STATE anomaly $\leftarrow$ \textsc{Stall}
    \ENDIF
    \IF{anomaly}
        \STATE $T_j \leftarrow \max(T_{\min},\; T_j - \delta_a)$
    \ELSE
        \STATE $T_j \leftarrow \min(1,\; T_j + \rho_p)$
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Frontier Assignment}
\label{sec:frontier}

All experimental conditions use the same frontier assignment to isolate the governance layer's contribution. The leader uses greedy nearest-frontier allocation~\cite{burgard2000collaborative}: robots are sorted by distance to their nearest frontier cell (preventing systematic ID bias), and each is assigned the nearest unassigned frontier with \emph{assignment persistence}---a robot retains its target until the cell is explored, preventing assignment thrashing that would cause inefficient zigzag movement.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental_design}
%=========================================================================

REIP was evaluated in both a physics-based simulation and a small-scale embedded hardware deployment. The simulation provides statistical scale for controlled comparison across fault conditions, while the hardware platform serves as a real-world validation testbed under sensing, communication, and localization noise.

\subsection{Simulation Protocol}

The simulation is implemented in PyGame and serves as the scalable evaluation environment for REIP. Robot processes run the full REIP/Raft node software, communicate via UDP, and evolve under time-based motion and wall-collision physics.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Arena}: multiroom layout shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:arena}, with a vertical interior wall at $x=1000$ and a top passage between the two rooms.
    \item \textbf{Robots}: 5 per trial, with seed-deterministic starting positions shared across controllers for paired comparison.
    \item \textbf{Duration}: 120\,s per trial.
    \item \textbf{Trials}: $N=100$ per condition.
    \item \textbf{Physics}: time-based motion at approximately $500\,$mm/s with wall-collision detection.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Communication}
\label{sec:communication}

Agents communicate via UDP broadcast at 5\,Hz. Each broadcast includes robot state (position, heading, trust scores), vote, visited cells (last 100), and leader assignments. Stale assignments older than 1\,s are automatically discarded. The broadcast rate determines the temporal resolution of fault detection: at 5\,Hz, the 2-command Tier~1 bound corresponds to 0.4\,s worst-case detection.

The hardware acts as a fixed validation testbed to prove the real world application of REIP under noisy and imperfect environments.

\subsection{Controllers}

We evaluate the proposed method against two reference baselines chosen to isolate leadership-fault resilience.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{REIP (Proposed)}: Three-tier proactive trust, merit-based election, democratic impeachment, governed fallback.
    \item \textbf{Raft Consensus (State-of-the-Art Baseline)}~\cite{ongaro2014raft}: Leader election via heartbeat-based liveness monitoring, where the leader periodically broadcasts a short ``I am alive'' message and followers trigger a new election if no heartbeat is received within a timeout window. Followers obey commands without trust verification. Raft is the dominant consensus protocol in production distributed systems but is \emph{not} Byzantine-fault-tolerant: it detects crashed leaders via heartbeat timeout but has no mechanism for detecting a live leader that issues degraded commands.
    \item \textbf{Decentralized (Reference)}: No leader; each robot independently explores using broadcast deconfliction with the same planner. Because no leader exists, leadership faults are inapplicable---this baseline establishes an upper bound on fault-immune but uncoordinated performance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Fault Conditions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Clean (NoFault)}: No faults injected. Establishes baseline performance and confirms that the trust model does not degrade efficiency when the leader is reliable.
    \item \textbf{Bad Leader (Byzantine Assignment)}: At $t{=}10\,$s, Robot~1 is compromised to persistently assign already-explored cells as targets. Followers are directed toward cells that provide zero new coverage. At $t{=}30\,$s, a second bad-leader fault targets the \emph{current} leader (typically a different robot, since Robot~1 was already impeached by REIP), testing repeated-attack survival. This directly exercises Tier~1 detection: followers recognize that the assigned cell is one they have personally visited.
    \item \textbf{Freeze Leader (Byzantine Stale Assignment)}: At $t{=}10\,$s, Robot~1 stops updating frontier assignments, caching and re-broadcasting the last valid assignment indefinitely. Followers are locked to targets that were relevant when the fault began but become stale as the environment is explored. At $t{=}30\,$s, a second freeze fault targets the current leader. Unlike Byzantine Assignment, the initial commands may appear reasonable---the assignments were valid when issued---so detection relies on Tier~1 accumulation as cached targets transition from unexplored to explored over time.
\end{itemize}

Both fault conditions target leadership specifically. This is a deliberate design choice: REIP's core contribution is trust-based governance for leader--follower systems. Leadership faults produce zero coverage impact on Decentralized (which has no leader) and catastrophic impact on Raft (which has no trust model). The dual sequential injection tests whether REIP can survive two consecutive Byzantine compromises in a single trial---and whether Raft can survive even one.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Controllers are evaluated under the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Final coverage}: percentage of cells explored by $t=120\,$s.
    \item \textbf{First suspicion time}: elapsed time from fault injection to the first nonzero trust-triggered suspicion event.
    \item \textbf{First impeachment time}: elapsed time from fault injection to the first completed impeachment.
    \item \textbf{Detection rate}: fraction of trials in which the injected faulty leader is successfully detected and impeached.
    \item \textbf{False positives}: number of trust-triggered impeachments in clean trials, excluding the initial election.
\end{itemize}

For summary reporting, trials with final coverage below 70\% are labeled \emph{catastrophic}, while trials with final coverage of 100\% are labeled \emph{perfect}.

We use two statistical functions to evaluate the resilience gap and the cumulative coverage efficiency.

\subsubsection{\textbf{Resilience gap}}
We define the \emph{resilience gap} $\Delta_R$ as the signed difference between clean and faulty coverage for a given controller:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_R = C_{\text{fault}} - C_{\text{clean}}
    \label{eq:resilience_gap}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\text{fault}}$ and $C_{\text{clean}}$ are final coverage under fault and clean conditions, respectively. A perfectly resilient system yields $\Delta_R = 0$; large negative $\Delta_R$ indicates the controller cannot recover from the fault.

\subsubsection{\textbf{Efficiency}}
To capture both convergence speed and final coverage in a single metric, we compute the \emph{cumulative coverage efficiency} $\eta$: the area under the coverage-vs-time curve normalized by trial duration,
\begin{equation}
    \eta = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^{T} C(t)\, dt
    \label{eq:efficiency}
\end{equation}
where $C(t) \in [0,1]$ is fractional coverage and $T = 120\,$s. Higher $\eta$ means faster, more sustained coverage.

\subsection{Dual Sequential Fault Survival}

The second fault injection at $t{=}30\,$s targets whoever is currently the leader---typically a \emph{different} robot from the first fault target, since Robot~1 was already impeached. REIP successfully impeaches the second compromised leader in 97 of 100 bad-leader trials and 99 of 100 freeze-leader trials, demonstrating that the trust model handles repeated Byzantine attacks across different fault mechanisms, not just single-shot recovery. For Raft, the second injection is a no-op: the system is already permanently compromised.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

To isolate the contribution of each REIP component, we ran an ablation study ($N{=}30$ per variant) under the bad-leader fault. Three variants were tested alongside REIP-Full:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No~Trust}: trust assessment and impeachment disabled; elections still occur but there is no detection mechanism.
    \item \textbf{No~Direction}: direction consistency check disabled; detection relies solely on the three-tier cell-level verification.
    \item \textbf{No~Causality}: causality grace period $\delta_b = 0$; any cell appearing in the visited set triggers immediate suspicion regardless of when it was explored.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{No~Trust} collapses to $39.7\%$ mean coverage with $0\%$ detection rate---\emph{worse} than Raft's $66.4\%$, because Raft occasionally recovers via incidental heartbeat disruptions while No~Trust has zero detection mechanism. This is the single most important ablation result: without trust-based detection, REIP offers no resilience advantage. Trust is not an optional enhancement; it is the entire mechanism.

\textbf{No~Direction} achieves $86.0\%$ coverage, a $4.9\%$ penalty vs.\ REIP-Full. The three-tier cell check alone detects bad-leader faults ($97\%$ detection rate), but the direction consistency check provides supplementary evidence that accelerates suspicion accumulation, explaining the coverage difference.

\textbf{No~Causality} achieves $96.1\%$ coverage under bad-leader---\emph{higher} than REIP-Full's $90.9\%$---because removing the grace period makes detection faster. However, this comes at a critical cost: $8.1$ false positives per trial under clean conditions (vs.\ $0.7$ for REIP-Full). A system that impeaches good leaders eight times per trial is operationally unusable despite high fault-condition coverage. Causality-aware verification is the mechanism that makes REIP \emph{deployable}: it suppresses false positives from concurrent exploration without meaningfully slowing detection of genuine faults.

In summary, the trust model is the single most critical component---without it, REIP has no fault resilience. The direction consistency check provides a modest but meaningful improvement, and causality gating is essential for operational reliability by keeping false positives below one per trial.

\subsection{What the Hardware Validation Means}

The hardware results do \emph{not} prove that REIP matches simulation in absolute throughput. They do show that the same governance logic remains functional under real sensing noise, localization noise, WiFi jitter, and actuator imperfections. The key validity claim is therefore \emph{transfer of relative behavior}, not equality of raw coverage numbers. REIP still preserves mission performance under hardware fault conditions, while Raft degrades substantially even in clean hardware trials.

\subsection{Proactive Detection and Resilience}
\label{sec:discussion_evolution}

The proactive model provides a structural advantage over reactive detectors: detection speed scales with evidence quality, not sample size. Reactive detectors require $O(1/\epsilon^2)$ observations to distinguish a faulty leader from noise; the proactive bound is $\lceil \sigma / (w_{\text{tier}} - r) \rceil$---just 2~commands under Tier~1 ground-truth evidence. The practical consequence is fault-invariant performance: REIP's median coverage gap between clean and both fault conditions is $0.0\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:mr_resilience}), and cumulative efficiency $\eta$ degrades by only $1.9\%$ under bad-leader attack. This invariance holds across both immediate misbehavior (bad-leader) and gradual staleness (freeze-leader).

\subsection{Why Leadership Matters}

Decentralized exploration avoids the single-point-of-failure problem but sacrifices coordination: agents operating on partial information inevitably duplicate coverage. A leader with a merged global map assigns non-overlapping frontiers and absorbs the cost of global map fusion, keeping per-agent compute lightweight on resource-constrained platforms. REIP preserves \emph{both} coordination quality and computational efficiency through rapid detection and recovery: it does not replace coordination with autonomy; it ensures that coordination \emph{survives} compromised leadership.

\subsection{Ablation Insights}

The No~Trust ablation collapses to $39.7\%$ coverage ($0\%$ detection)---worse than Raft---demonstrating that trust assessment is the core mechanism, not an optional enhancement. No~Causality achieves $96.1\%$ fault-condition coverage but at $8.1$ false positives per clean trial, rendering it operationally unusable. Causality-aware verification is what makes the trust signal reliable enough to deploy.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study is limited to 2D environments, frontier-based exploration, and leader-centric fault models. The restriction to 2D means that the evidence tiers and detection bounds may not directly transfer to 3D volumetric exploration, where occlusion and vertical motion introduce qualitatively different verification challenges. Hardware validation uses a small arena with five homogeneous robots; the three-tier confidence weights are calibrated for this scale, and larger deployments with higher broadcast latency may require re-tuned peer weights to maintain the same false-positive rate. Simulation fault profiles are predetermined rather than dynamically generated, meaning adversarial strategies that adapt to the trust model in real time remain untested.

Some seeds produce catastrophic trials for all controllers ($11/100$ for REIP under bad-leader), driven by initial position configurations that cause robots to wedge against walls. These failures are not governance-related but inflate variance, and a more sophisticated low-level path planner would likely eliminate them.

We do not prove that REIP converges to a stable leader in all cases. Under pathological conditions (e.g., all agents equally faulty), the election mechanism may cycle, degrading coordination. We mitigate this empirically with cooldown periods and the leader-failure counter in tie-breaking, and note that the worst-case detection bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:detection_bound}) holds regardless of convergence---each follower's detection is independent and bounded.

The hardware sample sizes remain small and uneven across conditions ($N{=}1{-}5$), so the hardware results should be interpreted as embedded feasibility and transfer validation rather than a statistically complete replacement for the simulation campaign. Expanding these trials is a priority for future work.

\subsection{Reproducibility}

All raw data, deterministic seeds, experiment scripts, and \LaTeX{} source are provided in a supplemental repository. Every figure and table in this paper can be regenerated from the provided seeds and harness scripts. The repository is available on USB at presentation.

\subsection{Broader Implications}

REIP's core insight---verifying leader \emph{quality}, not just leader \emph{liveness}---applies beyond multi-robot exploration to any leader--follower system where followers cannot independently verify the optimality of leader commands. Raft-family protocols detect crashed leaders via heartbeat timeout but assume that a live leader issues correct decisions; this assumption holds in log-replication systems (where entries are deterministically verifiable) but breaks in domains with partial observability and no ground-truth oracle.

Autonomous vehicle platoons, federated learning coordinators, and multi-drone survey missions all share this structure: a coordinator issues task assignments that followers cannot fully verify against a known-correct answer. A platoon leader whose perception is degraded by sensor fouling, or a federated learning aggregator subtly poisoning gradient updates, would remain undetected by heartbeat monitoring. REIP's governance architecture---proactive per-follower verification against local knowledge, confidence-weighted suspicion accumulation, and democratic impeachment---provides a template for these domains, offering sub-second detection with tunable sensitivity that adapts to mission-specific risk tolerance.

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Future work includes extending REIP to dynamic and zero-knowledge environments, heterogeneous teams, broader fault models (including faulty followers and adversarial agents manipulating trust scores of other agents), LLM based leaders, and validation on physical multi-robot platforms.

\textbf{Modular Systems}: Unique future deployment in a hybrid UAV-UGV system remains attractive as this can extend REIP beyond ground vehicles to modular heterogeneous cooperative robots~\cite{li2025drone}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=========================================================================

This paper introduced REIP, a trust-based governance framework that enables multi-robot teams to detect, impeach, and replace compromised leaders without centralized oversight or ground-truth oracle access.

The core mechanism is a proactive three-tier trust model that verifies leader commands \emph{before execution} against local knowledge ranked by source reliability. This yields a closed-form detection bound of $\lceil \sigma / (w_{\text{tier}} - r) \rceil$ commands---2~under ground-truth evidence ($w_1{=}1.0$), 8~under peer reports ($w_3{=}0.3$)---enabling sub-second fault detection without wasting physical movement on bad commands.

In multiroom experiments ($N{=}100$ per condition), REIP achieves:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $100\%$ median coverage under both clean and adversarial conditions---fault-invariant performance;
    \item Median fault detection in $0.20\,$s, impeachment in $1.21\,$s (bad-leader) and $1.67\,$s (freeze-leader), with $97{-}99\%$ detection rate;
    \item $+24.5\%$ mean coverage advantage over Raft under Byzantine Assignment ($90.9\%$ vs.\ $66.4\%$) and $+32.2\%$ under Freeze Leader ($96.1\%$ vs.\ $63.9\%$);
    \item Survival of two sequential Byzantine compromises in a single trial;
    \item Ablation confirmation that removing the trust model collapses coverage to $39.7\%$, confirming it is the primary mechanism.
\end{itemize}

REIP addresses a failure mode that existing approaches cannot: a leader that is alive, communicating, and issuing degraded commands. The state-of-the-art Raft protocol detects only crash failures via heartbeat timeout. Decentralized exploration avoids the problem by abandoning coordination entirely. REIP preserves the coordination advantage of hierarchical leadership while making that advantage resilient to arbitrary leadership faults through rapid, principled detection and democratic recovery.

\begin{abstract}
%=========================================================================
Multi-robot teams are increasingly deployed for exploration, mapping, and coverage tasks where coordinated groups outperform individual efforts. Within these teams, leader-follower architectures offer efficient coordination through centralized task allocation, but introduce a critical vulnerability: if the leader becomes compromised and issues faulty commands, mission quality degrades rapidly. This is especially dangerous in time-critical missions such as avalanche search-and-rescue, where delays waste coverage, endangering lives. This study investigated whether a robot team could detect and remove a faulty leader without centralized supervision or access to a ground-truth map.
The study proposes the Resilient Election and Impeachment Policy (REIP), a trust-based governance framework in which follower robots verify leader commands before execution using three levels of local evidence: personal visit history, sensor readings, and peer-reports. When commands conflict with local evidence, suspicion accumulates through a trust ledger. If trust falls below a threshold, followers vote to impeach the leader and elect a replacement. REIP was evaluated in a physics-based simulation running 100 trials per condition across three controllers and multiple fault types, then validated on a five-robot embedded hardware platform under real-world sensor noise.
Under both normal and faulty-leader conditions, REIP maintained full median coverage, sub-second fault detection, and replaced bad leaders within two seconds. Under faulty conditions, REIP outperformed a heartbeat-only baseline by over 24 percentage points in mean coverage. An ablation study confirmed removing the trust model collapsed coverage below 40\%. Results demonstrate that proactive trust-based governance makes coordinated robot teams resilient to compromised leadership in time-critical missions.



\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%=========================================================================

\subsection{Motivation and Context}
Multi-agent teams are increasingly deployed for exploration, mapping, and coverage tasks, where coordinated groups can survey larger areas more efficiently than individual agents. A subset of these missions — including disaster response, underground mapping, and hazardous-environment inspection — are extremely time-critical, imposing stricter requirements on coverage speed, fault tolerance, and communication reliability \cite{queralta2020sar, murphy2004rescue}. Avalanche and wilderness search-and-rescue exemplifies this class of mission: survival probability decreases sharply with delay, meaning even just a few extra minutes of slowdown defines life or death \cite{rauch2024avalanche}. Robot search teams could survey hazardous terrain, avalanche debris, or unstable environments more quickly than human responders alone while reducing rescuer exposure to danger.

 
For exploration and search tasks, hierarchical leader-follower coordination offers important advantages over purely decentralized behavior. A designated leader coordinates these teams by allocating search zones, preventing redundant sweeps, and maintaining a global picture of coverage progress \cite{yamauchi1997frontier, burgard2000collaborative, simmons2000coordination}. However, this leads to a major vulnerability: if a leader becomes compromised or experiences a fault, the rest of the team will continue to follow poor commands, wasting time, energy, and coverage speed.

Beyond search-and-rescue, the same vulnerability appears wherever autonomous teams operate without human oversight. Autonomous mining fleets already require resilient coordination when individual vehicles degrade~\cite{marshall2016robotics}, but these deployments are only viable if the system can survive equipment failures autonomously. The DARPA Subterranean Challenge confirmed that coordinated multi-robot teams consistently outperform single platforms in underground mapping~\cite{chung2023darpa}, and NASA's CADRE mission will deploy cooperative autonomous rovers on the lunar surface where communication blackouts make centralized human control infeasible~\cite{delacroix2024cadre, whittaker2012lunar}. On Mars, one-way communication delays of 4--24~minutes eliminate human-in-the-loop oversight entirely; robot teams must self-govern \cite{mcbrayer2022comm}.
 


\subsection{Problem Statement}

Leader--follower coordination improves efficiency in many multi-robot systems, but it also creates a critical single point of failure: if the leader becomes faulty or compromised, the rest of the team may continue executing harmful commands. In communication-limited or hostile environments where agents may be subject to intentional interference, this failure can arise from sensor degradation, intentional manipulation, software faults, or erroneous outputs from AI-based decision systems \cite{douceur2002sybil, ji2023hallucination}. A compromised leader does not need to crash to cause mission failure; it only needs to remain active while issuing low-quality or misleading assignments.


Distributed consensus protocols enable groups of networked agents to agree on shared state (e.g., who is the leader, what is the current map) even when individual agents may fail or misbehave. A \emph{Byzantine fault} occurs when an agent exhibits arbitrary, potentially malicious behavior---not just crashing or stopping, but actively sending conflicting or misleading information to different peers. Classical Byzantine fault-tolerant (BFT) protocols provide strong guarantees against such arbitrary faults, including malicious behavior, but assume reliable all-to-all communication and honest supermajorities---assumptions that are often unrealistic in range-limited robotic teams where communication is lossy and peer-to-peer \cite{lamport1982byzantine, castro1999practical}. Raft, a widely used consensus protocol for crash-tolerant distributed systems, detects leader failure through heartbeat timeout \cite{ongaro2014raft}. However, a leader that continues transmitting heartbeats while issuing degraded commands remains invisible to Raft. In parallel, frontier-based exploration methods typically assume that agents act in good faith \cite{yamauchi1997frontier, burgard2000collaborative}, and existing computational trust frameworks are rarely coupled with explicit impeachment-and-replacement mechanisms for robotic teams \cite{marsh1994formalising}.

In search-and-rescue missions, avoidable time delay and error is unacceptable. The core problem, therefore, is how a robot team can detect and remove a leader that is still alive and communicating, but no longer making trustworthy decisions, before catastrophic failure occurs.

\subsection{Proposed Solution}

This paper proposes the Resilient Election and Impeachment Policy (REIP), a trust-based governance framework for multi-robot teams. REIP enables followers to detect compromised leaders, impeach them through distributed voting, and elect replacements without centralized oversight or access to a ground-truth oracle.

The core mechanism is a proactive trust model in which each robot verifies leader commands before execution against its own local knowledge. REIP uses three-tier confidence-weighted suspicion accumulation, ranking evidence by source reliability: personal experience outweighs sensor readings, which outweigh peer reports. This allows the system to respond quickly to high-confidence faults while reducing false positives caused by stale or indirect information. Preliminary experimentation of REIP using a CUSUM--Bayesian fault detection~\cite{page1954cusum, gelman2013bayesian} found that reactive fault tolerance models only provide a partial solution because they attempt to remove faulty leaders after damage has already successfully occurred to a mission objective. This introduces detection latency that wastes physical time, battery, and coverage \cite{chandola2009anomaly}.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item A trust-based governance framework for multi-robot teams that enables distributed leader fault detection, democratic impeachment, and merit-based leader replacement without centralized oversight or ground-truth oracle access.
    \item A proactive three-tier confidence-weighted trust model that verifies leader commands before execution using local knowledge ranked by source reliability, together with a closed-form worst-case detection bound.
    \item A causality-aware verification mechanism that suppresses false positives from concurrent exploration by comparing visited-cell timestamps against leader assignment times.
    \item A rigorous evaluation with $N{=}100$ trials per condition, comparing REIP against Raft and decentralized baselines under dual sequential Byzantine fault injection, plus an ablation study quantifying the role of each major component.
    \item A proactive verification approach that operates under sparse, lossy, and intermittent peer-to-peer communication without requiring reliable all-to-all connectivity or honest supermajorities, making it suitable for range-limited robotic teams where classical Byzantine fault-tolerant protocols are impractical.
    \item Hardware validation on a five-robot embedded platform (\$15 Raspberry Pi Zero~2W, 512\,MB RAM per robot; \$514 total system cost) with overhead ArUco-based localization~\cite{garrido2014aruco}, demonstrating that Byzantine-resilient governance is achievable on ultra-low-cost hardware.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
%=========================================================================
REIP lies at the intersection of Byzantine fault tolerance, multi-robot frontier exploration, and trust-based coordination in multi-agent systems. We briefly review each area and highlight the gap that motivates our approach.

\subsection{Byzantine Fault Tolerance and Distributed Governance}

Classical BFT protocols provide strong guarantees against arbitrary faults under assumptions of reliable communication and honest supermajorities~\cite{lamport1982byzantine}. Practical protocols such as PBFT relax some synchrony assumptions~\cite{castro1999practical}, and the widely used Raft protocol provides leader election and log replication for crash-tolerant distributed systems~\cite{ongaro2014raft}. However, these assumptions are often difficult to satisfy in range-limited robot teams.

Raft is a consensus protocol designed for crash-tolerant distributed systems, where the primary failure mode is a leader that stops responding. It uses heartbeat-based leader election: if followers do not receive heartbeats from the leader within a timeout period, they initiate a new election to select a replacement. Raft has been widely adopted in production systems (e.g., etcd, Kubernetes) because it provides strong consistency guarantees under crash failures. However, Raft's detection mechanism is fundamentally limited: it can only detect leaders that have crashed or stopped communicating. A leader that continues sending heartbeats while issuing degraded or harmful commands remains invisible to Raft's timeout-based detection, leaving the team under indefinite faulty leadership.

REIP adopts the spirit of BFT --explicitly modeling faulty leaders and governing their authority --but operates under partial observability and lossy communication. Unlike Raft, which detects leader failure only via heartbeat timeout~\cite{ongaro2014raft}, REIP incorporates task-level performance reasoning into its leader assessment. This distinction is critical: a leader that is alive and communicating but issuing degraded commands (e.g., from sensor hallucination or adversarial compromise) will never trigger Raft's timeout-based detection, leaving the team under indefinite faulty leadership.

\subsection{Multi-Robot Frontier Exploration}

Frontier-based exploration assigns robots to boundaries between known and unknown space~\cite{yamauchi1997frontier}, with multi-robot extensions coordinating assignments to maximize coverage rate~\cite{burgard2000collaborative}. Leader--follower architectures exploit a global view for coordinated assignment~\cite{simmons2000coordination}, but treat the leader as implicitly trustworthy. There is no mechanism for detecting when the leader's directions become counterproductive, nor for replacing the leader without external supervision. REIP augments a standard frontier-based leader--follower stack with a governance layer that monitors, impeaches, and replaces a failing leader based on observed performance---giving each agent the autonomy to question orders and, collectively, to act on that judgment.

\subsection{Trust and Reputation in Multi-Agent Systems}

Computational trust frameworks quantify agent reliability from observed behavior~\cite{marsh1994formalising}, and have been applied in peer-to-peer networks, e-commerce, and multi-agent simulations~\cite{ramchurn2004trust}. However, most trust models assume access to ground-truth outcomes: a buyer can verify whether the product was delivered correctly. In multi-robot exploration under partial observability, ground truth is not directly available; agents observe only local coverage changes and peer broadcasts. This gap motivates a belief-only trust approach that operates without an oracle map --- the design described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}. Rather than measuring trust from outcomes (which require following potentially bad commands), the proactive model verifies leader commands against local knowledge before execution, enabling faster fault detection, improved robustness to compromised leaders, and more efficient resource utilization. This design makes it suitable for both simulation and resource-constrained hardware platforms.

\section{REIP Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%=========================================================================

REIP operates as a governance layer atop a frontier-based leader--follower exploration stack. Each robot maintains a high-resolution local map of personally observed cells, a low-resolution global belief map aggregated from peer broadcasts, and a coverage/motion history. Robots share compressed belief updates and state via peer-to-peer broadcast. The leader assigns frontiers using a common exploration policy. Followers verify those assignments against local evidence before execution. When trust falls below threshold, the team impeaches the leader and elects a replacement. The following subsections detail the verification pipeline, detection mechanics, and governance procedures. Table~\ref{tab:notation} consolidates the notation used throughout this section. Fig.~\ref{fig:flowchart} shows the entire pipeline of processing commands from receiving to the trust update.

\subsection{Information Zones}
\label{sec:info_zones}

Each robot maintains two overlapping information zones that determine what it can verify about a leader's command:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-resolution zone}: cells the robot has personally visited. This is absolute ground truth regardless of the robot's current position or distance from those cells---the memory of having been there is permanent.
    \item \textbf{Local zone}: cells currently within time-of-flight (ToF) sensor range ($200\,$mm). Obstacle readings here are instantaneous physical measurements that are regenerated each cycle and do not accumulate.

\end{itemize}

Beyond these zones, the robot relies on the low-resolution global belief map built from peer broadcasts. This data may be stale: a cell reported as unexplored 2 seconds ago may have been visited by another robot since the last broadcast. The three-tier model weights evidence accordingly.

\subsection{Three-Tier Evidence Verification}
\label{sec:hw_trust}

When the leader assigns a target cell, the follower checks it against three tiers of local knowledge. We formalize the ordering as follows.

\begin{definition}[Evidence Ordering]
\label{def:evidence}
Evidence sources are ordered by reliability:
\[
    \text{Tier\,1\,(personal)} \;\succ\;
    \text{Tier\,2\,(sensor)} \;\succ\;
    \text{Tier\,3\,(peer)},
\]
with confidence weights $w_1 = w_2 = 1.0 > w_3 = 0.3$.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Tier~1}: the assigned cell is in $\mathcal{V}_i$; the ground-truth. Harmful commands are more obvious at this tier.
    \item \textbf{Tier~2}: the assigned cell is within ToF range and contains a detected obstacle (sensor validation). This also prevents forced collision between peer follower robots.
    \item \textbf{Tier~3}: the assigned cell has been reported as explored by another robot's broadcast and map merge, but robot~$i$ has not visited it personally (the report may be stale due to broadcast delay).
\end{itemize}
Higher-confidence evidence yields a tighter detection bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:detection}).
\end{definition}

Let $w_t \defeq w(c_t) + w_d(\alpha_t,\, w(c_t)) + w_{\Omega}(\Omega_t)$ denote the total evidence weight at cycle~$t$, including the direction-consistency bonus (Section~\ref{sec:direction_consistency}) and command-instability check (Section~\ref{sec:command_instability}).

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Three-Tier Evidence Weighting}
\label{alg:evidence}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Target cell $c$, assignment time $t_a$
\ENSURE Evidence weight $w$
\STATE $w \leftarrow 0$
\IF{$c \in \texttt{my\_visited}$ \AND $t_{\text{visit}}(c) < t_a - \delta_b$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 1.0$
    \COMMENT{Tier~1: personal ground truth}
\ELSIF{$c$ within ToF range \AND obstacle at $c$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 1.0$
    \COMMENT{Tier~2: sensor verification}
\ELSIF{$c \in \texttt{known\_visited} \setminus \texttt{my\_visited}$
       \AND $t_{\text{known}}(c) < t_a - \delta_b$}
    \STATE $w \leftarrow 0.3$
    \COMMENT{Tier~3: peer-reported}
\ENDIF
\RETURN $w$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Trust Dynamics}
\label{sec:trust_dynamics}

\begin{definition}[Trust State Machine]
\label{def:trust_sm}
The per-follower trust state $(S_t, T_t)\in\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\times[T_{\min},1]$ evolves under the transition
\begin{align}
S_{t+1} &\;=\; \clip{\,S_t + w_t - r\,\Ind[w_t=0]\,},
\label{eq:suspicion_compact}\\
k_{t+1} &\;\defeq\; \left\lfloor S_{t+1}\,/\,\sigma \right\rfloor,
\label{eq:crossings}\\
T_{t+1} &\;=\; \max\!\bigl(T_{\min},\;
         T_t - k_{t+1}\,\Delta_{\text{decay}}\bigr),
\label{eq:trust_compact}\\
S_{t+1} &\;\leftarrow\; S_{t+1} - k_{t+1}\,\sigma.
\label{eq:residual}
\end{align}
Impeachment triggers when $T_{t+1} < \tau_{\text{imp}}$.
\end{definition}

The state machine implements a time-varying trust decay model: suspicion $S_t$ accumulates incrementally with each command's evidence weight $w_t$, while trust $T_t$ decays discretely whenever suspicion crosses the threshold $\sigma$. Specifically, each command adds its evidence weight to suspicion; when suspicion reaches or exceeds $\sigma$, trust decreases by $\Delta_{\text{decay}}$ and suspicion resets by subtracting $\sigma$ (preserving any residual above the threshold). This creates a stepwise decay trajectory: trust remains stable during normal operation but drops in discrete steps as evidence accumulates, providing a clear signal when a leader's behavior degrades. The state machine is a deterministic recurrence: given the evidence stream $(w_0, w_1, \ldots)$, the trajectory of $(S_t, T_t)$ is uniquely determined. This enables the closed-form detection bound derived in Theorem~\ref{thm:detection}. Direct verification was chosen over reactive statistical accumulation because at hardware scale (192 cells, five robots) every relevant fault mode is caught faster.


\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Suspicion Update and Trust Decay}
\label{alg:suspicion}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Total weight $w_{\Sigma} = w + w_d + w_{\Omega}$
\IF{$w_{\Sigma} > 0$}
    \STATE $S \leftarrow S + w_{\Sigma}$
\ELSE
    \STATE $S \leftarrow \max(0,\; S - r)$
    \COMMENT{Decay toward zero}
\ENDIF
\WHILE{$S \geq \sigma$}
    \STATE $T_{\text{leader}} \leftarrow \max(T_{\min},\;
           T_{\text{leader}} - \Delta_{\text{decay}})$
    \STATE $S \leftarrow S - \sigma$
    \COMMENT{Carry-over residual}
\ENDWHILE
\IF{$T_{\text{leader}} < \tau_{\text{imp}}$}
    \STATE Trigger impeachment $\to$ Algorithm~\ref{alg:election}
\ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Detection Bound}
\label{sec:detection_bound}

The deterministic structure of Definition~\ref{def:trust_sm} yields a closed-form bound on how quickly a Byzantine leader is detected and removed.

\begin{theorem}[Worst-Case Detection Bound]
\label{thm:detection}
Under persistent adversarial commands verified with tier-$k$ evidence weight~$w_k > r$, the first trust decay occurs within
\begin{equation}
    n_{\text{detect}} = \left\lceil \frac{\sigma}{w_k - r}
    \right\rceil
    \label{eq:detection_bound}
\end{equation}
commands. Full impeachment occurs within
\begin{equation}
    n_{\text{imp}} = \underbrace{\left\lceil
    \frac{T_0 - \tau_{\text{imp}}}{\Delta_{\text{decay}}}
    \right\rceil}_{k_{\text{imp}}\text{ crossings}} \;\cdot\;
    n_{\text{detect}}
    \label{eq:impeachment_bound}
\end{equation}
commands.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Each bad command increases suspicion by at least $w_k - r > 0$ (since $w_k \geq w_3 = 0.3 > r = 0.1$ by Definition~\ref{def:evidence}). The threshold~$\sigma$ is therefore crossed after at most $\lceil\sigma/(w_k - r)\rceil$ commands; each crossing decrements trust by~$\Delta_{\text{decay}}$. The number of crossings required to reach~$\tau_{\text{imp}}$ is $k_{\text{imp}} = \lceil(T_0 - \tau_{\text{imp}})/\Delta_{\text{decay}}\rceil$; the product follows.
\end{proof}

\noindent\textbf{Instantiation.}\;
Under Tier~1 evidence ($w_1{=}1.0$, $r{=}0.1$, $\sigma{=}1.5$): $n_{\text{detect}} = \lceil 1.5/0.9 \rceil = 2$~commands. Under Tier~3 alone ($w_3{=}0.3$): $n_{\text{detect}} = \lceil 1.5/0.2 \rceil = 8$~commands. With $\Delta_{\text{decay}}{=}0.2$ and $\tau_{\text{imp}}{=}0.3$, $k_{\text{imp}} = 4$, yielding $n_{\text{imp}} = 8$ (Tier~1) or $32$ (Tier~3) commands to full impeachment. The four-fold gap between tiers directly encodes the epistemological difference between ground truth and peer hearsay.

\begin{corollary}[Liveness]
\label{cor:liveness}
If a Byzantine leader generates at least one flaggable command every $\Delta t$~seconds, impeachment occurs within $n_{\text{imp}} \cdot \Delta t$~seconds. At the 5\,Hz broadcast rate, Tier-1 evidence yields impeachment within $8 \times 0.2\,\text{s} = 1.6\,$s---consistent with the empirical median of 1.21--1.67\,s reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
\end{corollary}

The sub-second to low-second impeachment times represent a favorable trade-off for time-critical missions: fast enough to prevent significant coverage loss (median $1.21$--$1.67$\,s under Tier~1 evidence) while remaining conservative enough to avoid false positives from transient network delays or sensor noise. The trust decay parameters ($\sigma$, $\Delta_{\text{decay}}$, $\tau_{\text{imp}}$) are tunable: increasing $\sigma$ or decreasing $\Delta_{\text{decay}}$ slows impeachment but reduces false positives; decreasing $\tau_{\text{imp}}$ accelerates removal but may trigger premature elections. The current values ($\sigma{=}1.5$, $\Delta_{\text{decay}}{=}0.2$, $\tau_{\text{imp}}{=}0.3$) were selected to balance detection speed against false-positive rate, achieving $0.7$ false impeachments per clean trial while maintaining sub-second detection under fault conditions.

\subsection{Causality Gating}
\label{sec:causality}

Concurrent exploration can cause false positives: a cell may be visited by a peer \emph{after} the leader computed its assignment but \emph{before} the follower verifies the command. A causality gate suppresses this by requiring that the evidence predate the assignment by at least~$\delta_b$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{flag}(c) = \mathbb{I}\bigl(c \in \texttt{known\_visited}
    \;\wedge\; t_{\text{known}}(c) < t_{\text{assign}} - \delta_b\bigr)
\end{equation}
where $\delta_b = 0.5\,$s (${\approx}\,2.5\times$ the 5\,Hz broadcast period). The gate applies to Tiers~1 and~3 (stored map evidence); Tier~2 uses instantaneous ToF data and needs no stale-data gating. A genuinely compromised leader assigns cells explored well outside this window, so detection is unaffected.

\subsection{Direction Consistency}
\label{sec:direction_consistency}

A complementary geometric check detects faults invisible to the map-based tiers---commands that point away from \emph{all} unexplored territory. The follower computes
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \bigl|\text{atan2}(\Delta y_f,
    \Delta x_f) - \text{atan2}(\Delta y_{\text{cmd}},
    \Delta x_{\text{cmd}})\bigr|,
\end{equation}
the best angular alignment between the command and any frontier cell (normalized to $[0,\pi]$). Two thresholds govern the response: $\alpha > 3\pi/4$ triggers standalone suspicion; $\pi/2 < \alpha \leq 3\pi/4$ reinforces only when the three-tier check has already flagged the command. This catches severe directional faults without penalizing legitimate distant assignments (see Algorithm~\ref{alg:direction}).

The per-command verification pipeline comprises four atomic steps: evidence weighting (Algorithm~\ref{alg:evidence}), direction consistency (Algorithm~\ref{alg:direction}), command instability (Algorithm~\ref{alg:instability}), and suspicion update (Algorithm~\ref{alg:suspicion}).

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Direction Consistency Check}
\label{alg:direction}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Command direction $\theta_c$, frontier set $\mathcal{F}$,
         evidence weight $w$
\ENSURE Direction suspicion $w_d$
\STATE $\alpha \leftarrow \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}}
       \lvert\theta_f - \theta_c\rvert$
\IF{$\alpha > 3\pi/4$}
    \STATE $w_d \leftarrow 0.5 + 0.3 \cdot
           \frac{\alpha - 3\pi/4}{\pi/4}$
    \COMMENT{Severe: all frontiers behind}
\ELSIF{$\alpha > \pi/2$ \AND $w > 0$}
    \STATE $w_d \leftarrow 0.3 \cdot
           \frac{\alpha - \pi/2}{\pi/4}$
    \COMMENT{Reinforcing only}
\ELSE
    \STATE $w_d \leftarrow 0$
\ENDIF
\RETURN $w_d$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Command Instability}
\label{sec:command_instability}

A temporal check detects oscillation attacks where a leader rapidly flip-flops between valid but conflicting targets, causing followers to waste time oscillating in place. Even if each individual command passes the three-tier check (e.g., alternating between two valid unexplored corners), the rapid direction changes indicate harmful behavior. The follower tracks the angular velocity of commanded directions over a sliding window:

\begin{equation}
    \Omega_t = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{W}|} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{W}} |\Delta\theta_i|,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{W}$ is an 8-command window and $\Delta\theta_i = |\theta_{\text{cmd}}(i) - \theta_{\text{cmd}}(i-1)|$ is the angular change between consecutive commands (normalized to $[0,\pi]$). High $\Omega$ indicates rapid command flipping: normal exploration yields $\Omega \approx 1.6$--$2.2$~rad, while oscillation attacks produce $\Omega \approx 2.5$--$3.0$~rad (180° flips every cycle).

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Command Instability Check}
\label{alg:instability}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Command direction $\theta_c$, command history $\mathcal{H}$, leader tenure $t_{\text{tenure}}$
\ENSURE Instability suspicion $w_{\Omega}$
\STATE $w_{\Omega} \leftarrow 0$
\IF{$|\mathcal{H}| \geq 8$ \AND $t_{\text{tenure}} \geq 2.0$}
    \STATE $\Omega \leftarrow \text{mean}(|\Delta\theta|)$ over last 8 commands
    \IF{$\Omega \geq 3\pi/4$}
        \STATE $\Omega_{\text{excess}} \leftarrow \frac{\Omega - 3\pi/4}{\pi - 3\pi/4}$
        \STATE $w_{\Omega} \leftarrow 0.3 \cdot \min(\Omega_{\text{excess}}, 1.0)$
        \COMMENT{Scales from 0 at threshold to 0.3 at $\Omega = \pi$}
    \ENDIF
\ENDIF
\RETURN $w_{\Omega}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The threshold $3\pi/4 \approx 2.356$~rad (135°) sits between normal exploration and oscillation attack distributions. A grace period of $2.0$~s after leader election prevents false positives during initial target reassignments. This check complements the three-tier and direction-consistency checks by detecting temporal patterns that individual command verification misses.

\subsection{Impeachment}
\label{sec:impeachment}

The preceding three-tier evidence verification (Algorithm~\ref{alg:evidence}), direction consistency (Algorithm~\ref{alg:direction}), command instability (Algorithm~\ref{alg:instability}), and causality gating, each contribute to the suspicion accumulator. That accumulated suspicion drives trust downward through the decay model (Algorithm~\ref{alg:suspicion}): each threshold crossing reduces trust by $\Delta_{\text{decay}}$, and when trust drops below the impeachment threshold $\tau_{\text{imp}}$, the follower triggers a vote to remove the current leader.

Formally, impeachment triggers when $T_{\text{leader}} < \tau_{\text{imp}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:trust_sm}). By Theorem~\ref{thm:detection}, this requires at most $n_{\text{imp}}$ adversarial commands: 8 under Tier-1 evidence, 32 under Tier-3 alone. A cool-down period after each election prevents oscillatory leader churn.


\subsection{Leader Election}
\label{sec:election}

Upon impeachment, a new leader is elected by democratic vote. Each robot votes for the highest-trust eligible candidate from its local trust ledger. The eligible candidate set is restricted to agents whose trust exceeds a voting threshold:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{E} = \bigl\{i : T_{\cdot,i} > T_{\text{thresh}}\bigr\}
    \label{eq:eligible_set}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{thresh}} = 0.5$. The new leader is the eligible candidate receiving the most votes:
\begin{equation}
    \text{leader}_{\text{new}} = \arg\max_{i \in \mathcal{E}} \;
    \bigl|\{j : v_j = i\}\bigr|
    \label{eq:election}
\end{equation}
where $v_j$ is robot~$j$'s vote. Ties are broken by a two-level comparator: (1)~fewest prior impeachments (tracked per-agent as $\text{failures}(i)$), (2)~lowest robot~ID. Trust is already encoded in the vote: each robot votes for the highest-trust eligible candidate, so aggregate trust influences the outcome through the voting process itself. The failure counter prevents a repeatedly impeached agent from cycling back into leadership through tie-breaking alone.

Upon election, trust in the new leader resets to 1.0 and suspicion resets to 0, providing a clean evaluation window. Non-leader peers recover trust gradually via
\begin{equation}
    T_{i,j}(t{+}1) = \min\bigl(1,\; T_{i,j}(t) + \rho\bigr),
    \quad j \neq \text{leader}
    \label{eq:trust_recovery}
\end{equation}
where $\rho = 0.005$ per broadcast cycle. This slow recovery ensures that a previously impeached agent can be re-elected after demonstrating sustained good behavior, but cannot ``wash out'' accumulated distrust from a few cycles of normality.

A \emph{fast-start} mechanism accelerates initial leader election: the first three elections occur at 0.5\,s intervals before switching to the normal 2.0\,s election interval. This eliminates the startup latency where robots sit idle waiting for the first election to converge.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Merit-Based Leader Election}
\label{alg:election}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Impeached leader $\ell$, trust ledger $\{T_{i,j}\}$
\ENSURE New leader $\ell_{\text{new}}$
\STATE $\mathcal{E} \leftarrow \{j : T_{\cdot,j} > T_{\text{thresh}}\}$
    \COMMENT{Eligible candidates}
\FOR{each robot $i$}
    \STATE $v_i \leftarrow \arg\max_{j \in \mathcal{E}} T_{i,j}$
    \COMMENT{Vote for highest-trust eligible}
\ENDFOR
\STATE $\text{tally}(j) \leftarrow |\{i : v_i = j\}| \;\forall\, j \in \mathcal{E}$
\STATE $\ell_{\text{new}} \leftarrow \arg\max_{j \in \mathcal{E}}
       \bigl(\text{tally}(j),\;
       {-}\text{failures}(j),\;
       {-}j\bigr)$
    \COMMENT{Votes $\to$ fewest failures $\to$ lowest ID}
\STATE $T_{\cdot,\ell_{\text{new}}} \leftarrow 1.0$;\;
       $S_{\ell_{\text{new}}} \leftarrow 0$
    \COMMENT{Clean slate}
\RETURN $\ell_{\text{new}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Peer Anomaly Detection}
\label{sec:peer_anomaly}

Non-leader peers are monitored for behavioral anomalies to prevent compromised agents from inheriting leadership:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stuck}: $< 50\,$mm movement over $\geq 2\,$s;
    \item \textbf{Spinning}: angular change $> \pi\,$rad with translation $< 100\,$mm over $\geq 1\,$s;
    \item \textbf{Coverage stall}: zero new cells over $\geq 5\,$s (evaluated only when the current leader is trusted, since a bad leader's commands cause all followers to stall).
\end{itemize}
Peer trust evolves as:
\begin{equation}
    T_{\text{peer}}(t{+}1) = \begin{cases}
        \max\bigl(T_{\min},\; T_{\text{peer}}(t) - \delta_a\bigr)
            & \text{if anomaly = true} \\[4pt]
        \min\bigl(1,\; T_{\text{peer}}(t) + \rho_p\bigr)
            & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:peer_trust}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_a = 0.02$ (anomaly penalty) and $\rho_p = 0.005$ (recovery rate). The asymmetric rates ($\delta_a / \rho_p = 4$) ensure that a single anomaly event takes four normal cycles to recover, preventing a briefly compromised agent from immediately regaining election eligibility.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Peer Anomaly Detection}
\label{alg:peer_anomaly}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\FOR{each non-leader peer $j$}
    \IF{$\lVert\Delta\mathbf{p}_j\rVert < 50\,$mm over $\geq 2\,$s}
        \STATE anomaly $\leftarrow$ \textsc{Stuck}
    \ELSIF{$|\Delta\theta_j| > \pi$ \AND
           $\lVert\Delta\mathbf{p}_j\rVert < 100\,$mm over $\geq 1\,$s}
        \STATE anomaly $\leftarrow$ \textsc{Spinning}
    \ELSIF{new cells $= 0$ over $\geq 5\,$s \AND leader trusted}
        \STATE anomaly $\leftarrow$ \textsc{Stall}
    \ENDIF
    \IF{anomaly}
        \STATE $T_j \leftarrow \max(T_{\min},\; T_j - \delta_a)$
    \ELSE
        \STATE $T_j \leftarrow \min(1,\; T_j + \rho_p)$
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Frontier Assignment}
\label{sec:frontier}

All experimental conditions use the same frontier assignment to isolate the governance layer's contribution. The leader uses greedy nearest-frontier allocation~\cite{burgard2000collaborative}: robots are sorted by distance to their nearest frontier cell (preventing systematic ID bias), and each is assigned the nearest unassigned frontier with \emph{assignment persistence}---a robot retains its target until the cell is explored, preventing assignment thrashing that would cause inefficient zigzag movement.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental_design}
%=========================================================================

REIP was evaluated in both a physics-based simulation and a small-scale embedded hardware deployment. The simulation provides statistical scale for controlled comparison across fault conditions, while the hardware platform serves as a real-world validation testbed under sensing, communication, and localization noise.

\subsection{Simulation Protocol}

The simulation is implemented in PyGame and serves as the scalable evaluation environment for REIP. Robot processes run the full REIP/Raft node software, communicate via UDP, and evolve under time-based motion and wall-collision physics.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Arena}: multiroom layout shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:arena}, with a vertical interior wall at $x=1000$ and a top passage between the two rooms.
    \item \textbf{Robots}: 5 per trial, with seed-deterministic starting positions shared across controllers for paired comparison.
    \item \textbf{Duration}: 120\,s per trial.
    \item \textbf{Trials}: $N=100$ per condition.
    \item \textbf{Physics}: time-based motion at approximately $500\,$mm/s with wall-collision detection.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware Setup}

The hardware acts as a fixed validation testbed to prove the real world application of REIP under noisy and imperfect environments.

\subsection{Hardware Design}
\label{sec:hw_arch}
The hardware platform consists of five custom-built differential-drive robots ($147 \times 128 \times 42\,$mm, $86.5\,$mm wheelbase). Each robot is built around a Raspberry Pi Zero 2W running the REIP node software, with a Raspberry Pi Pico handling low-level motor control via UART. Movement is provided by two N20 geared DC motors (1:100 ratio) with magnetic encoders, and a rear ball caster for stability.  Each robot carries five VL53L0X time-of-flight (ToF) sensors on a TCA9548A I2C multiplexer, providing $200\,$mm range obstacle detection. See Table~\ref{tab:bom} for the price and quantity of each component used per-robot. See Fig.~\ref{fig:quad_gallery} for detailed images of the fully assembled robot.

\subsection{Communication}
\label{sec:communication}

Agents communicate via UDP broadcast at 5\,Hz. Each broadcast includes robot state (position, heading, trust scores), vote, visited cells (last 100), and leader assignments. Stale assignments older than 1\,s are automatically discarded. The broadcast rate determines the temporal resolution of fault detection: at 5\,Hz, the 2-command Tier~1 bound corresponds to 0.4\,s worst-case detection.

\subsection{Controllers}

We evaluate the proposed method against two reference baselines chosen to isolate leadership-fault resilience.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{REIP (Proposed)}: Three-tier proactive trust, merit-based election, democratic impeachment, governed fallback.
    \item \textbf{Raft Consensus (State-of-the-Art Baseline)}~\cite{ongaro2014raft}: Heartbeat-based leader election; followers obey commands without trust verification. Raft is the dominant consensus protocol in production distributed systems but is \emph{not} Byzantine-fault-tolerant: it detects crashed leaders via heartbeat timeout but has no mechanism for detecting a live leader that issues degraded commands.
    \item \textbf{Decentralized (Reference)}: No leader; each robot independently explores using broadcast deconfliction with the same planner. Because no leader exists, leadership faults are inapplicable---this baseline establishes an upper bound on fault-immune but uncoordinated performance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Fault Conditions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Clean (NoFault)}: No faults injected. Establishes baseline performance and confirms that the trust model does not degrade efficiency when the leader is reliable.
    \item \textbf{Bad Leader (Byzantine Assignment)}: At $t{=}10\,$s, Robot~1 is compromised to persistently assign already-explored cells as targets. Followers are directed toward cells that provide zero new coverage. At $t{=}30\,$s, a second bad-leader fault targets the \emph{current} leader (typically a different robot, since Robot~1 was already impeached by REIP), testing repeated-attack survival. This directly exercises Tier~1 detection: followers recognize that the assigned cell is one they have personally visited.
    \item \textbf{Freeze Leader (Byzantine Stale Assignment)}: At $t{=}10\,$s, Robot~1 stops updating frontier assignments, caching and re-broadcasting the last valid assignment indefinitely. Followers are locked to targets that were relevant when the fault began but become stale as the environment is explored. At $t{=}30\,$s, a second freeze fault targets the current leader. Unlike Byzantine Assignment, the initial commands may appear reasonable---the assignments were valid when issued---so detection relies on Tier~1 accumulation as cached targets transition from unexplored to explored over time.
\end{itemize}

Both fault conditions target leadership specifically. This is a deliberate design choice: REIP's core contribution is trust-based governance for leader--follower systems. Leadership faults produce zero coverage impact on Decentralized (which has no leader) and catastrophic impact on Raft (which has no trust model). The dual sequential injection tests whether REIP can survive two consecutive Byzantine compromises in a single trial---and whether Raft can survive even one.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Controllers are evaluated under the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Final coverage}: percentage of cells explored by $t=120\,$s.
    \item \textbf{First suspicion time}: elapsed time from fault injection to the first nonzero trust-triggered suspicion event.
    \item \textbf{First impeachment time}: elapsed time from fault injection to the first completed impeachment.
    \item \textbf{Detection rate}: fraction of trials in which the injected faulty leader is successfully detected and impeached.
    \item \textbf{False positives}: number of trust-triggered impeachments in clean trials, excluding the initial election.
\end{itemize}

For summary reporting, trials with final coverage below 70\% are labeled \emph{catastrophic}, while trials with final coverage of 100\% are labeled \emph{perfect}.

We use two statistical functions to evaluate the resilience gap and the cumulative coverage efficiency.

\subsubsection{\textbf{Resilience gap}}
We define the \emph{resilience gap} $\Delta_R$ as the signed difference between clean and faulty coverage for a given controller:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_R = C_{\text{fault}} - C_{\text{clean}}
    \label{eq:resilience_gap}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\text{fault}}$ and $C_{\text{clean}}$ are final coverage under fault and clean conditions, respectively. A perfectly resilient system yields $\Delta_R = 0$; large negative $\Delta_R$ indicates the controller cannot recover from the fault.

\subsubsection{\textbf{Efficiency}}
To capture both convergence speed and final coverage in a single metric, we compute the \emph{cumulative coverage efficiency} $\eta$: the area under the coverage-vs-time curve normalized by trial duration,
\begin{equation}
    \eta = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^{T} C(t)\, dt
    \label{eq:efficiency}
\end{equation}
where $C(t) \in [0,1]$ is fractional coverage and $T = 120\,$s. Higher $\eta$ means faster, more sustained coverage.

After the freeze, Raft followers permanently chase stale targets that were valid when cached but become useless as the environment is explored; coverage plateaus and never recovers. REIP achieves $96.1\%$ mean coverage ($100.0\%$ median) with only $2/100$ catastrophic trials---detection rate $99/100$, median first suspicion in $0.20\,$s, median impeachment in $1.67\,$s. Because freeze-leader produces initially valid assignments that \emph{gradually} become stale, Tier~1 violations accumulate as cached targets transition from unexplored to personally visited, confirming that the three-tier model detects both immediate and delayed Byzantine misbehavior.

REIP's freeze-leader mean ($96.1\%$) slightly exceeds its clean mean ($91.2\%$), though both achieve identical median coverage ($100\%$). This mean difference reflects statistical variance rather than a fundamental performance advantage: freeze-leader has fewer catastrophic trials ($2/100$ vs.\ $11/100$ for clean), which pulls the mean upward. The freeze-leader condition benefits from initially valid cached assignments that provide coverage before becoming stale, and fast impeachment ($1.67$\,s median) re-elects a known-good leader with minimal overhead. The key result is fault-invariant median performance: $100\%$ coverage in both clean and fault conditions, demonstrating that REIP's governance layer successfully absorbs the fault without measurable degradation.

\subsection{Proactive Detection and Resilience}
\label{sec:discussion_evolution}

The proactive model provides a structural advantage over reactive detectors: detection speed scales with evidence quality, not sample size. Reactive detectors require $O(1/\epsilon^2)$ observations to distinguish a faulty leader from noise; the proactive bound is $\lceil \sigma / (w_{\text{tier}} - r) \rceil$---just 2~commands under Tier~1 ground-truth evidence. The practical consequence is fault-invariant performance: REIP's median coverage gap between clean and both fault conditions is $0.0\,\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:mr_resilience}), and cumulative efficiency $\eta$ degrades by only $1.9\,$pp under bad-leader attack. This invariance holds across both immediate misbehavior (bad-leader) and gradual staleness (freeze-leader).

\subsection{Why Leadership Matters}

Decentralized exploration avoids the single-point-of-failure problem but sacrifices coordination: agents operating on partial information inevitably duplicate coverage. A leader with a merged global map assigns non-overlapping frontiers and absorbs the cost of global map fusion, keeping per-agent compute lightweight on resource-constrained platforms. REIP preserves \emph{both} coordination quality and computational efficiency through rapid detection and recovery: it does not replace coordination with autonomy; it ensures that coordination \emph{survives} compromised leadership.

\subsection{Ablation Insights}

The No~Trust ablation collapses to $39.7\%$ coverage ($0\%$ detection)---worse than Raft---demonstrating that trust assessment is the core mechanism, not an optional enhancement. No~Causality achieves $96.1\%$ fault-condition coverage but at $8.1$ false positives per clean trial, rendering it operationally unusable. Causality-aware verification is what makes the trust signal reliable enough to deploy.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study is limited to 2D environments, frontier-based exploration, and leader-centric fault models. Hardware validation uses a small arena with five homogeneous robots. The three-tier confidence weights are calibrated for this scale; larger deployments with higher broadcast latency may require adjusted peer weights. Simulation fault profiles are predetermined rather than live LLM-generated. Parameters are fixed rather than adaptively tuned.

Some seeds produce catastrophic trials for all controllers ($11/100$ for REIP under bad-leader), driven by initial position configurations that cause robots to wedge against walls. These failures are not governance-related but reflect limitations in the low-level path planner.

We do not prove that REIP converges to a stable leader in all cases. Under pathological conditions (e.g., all agents equally faulty), the election mechanism may cycle. We mitigate this empirically with cooldown periods and the leader-failure counter in tie-breaking, and note that the worst-case detection bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:detection_bound}) holds regardless of convergence---each follower's detection is independent and bounded.

The hardware sample sizes remain small and uneven across conditions. That means the hardware results should be interpreted as embedded feasibility and transfer validation, not as a statistically complete replacement for the simulation campaign.

\subsection{Reproducibility}

All raw data, deterministic seeds, experiment scripts, and \LaTeX{} source are provided in a supplemental repository. Every figure and table in this paper can be regenerated from the provided seeds and harness scripts. The repository is available on USB at presentation.

\subsection{Broader Implications}

REIP's core insight---verifying leader \emph{quality}, not just leader \emph{liveness}---applies beyond multi-robot exploration to any leader--follower system where followers cannot independently verify the optimality of leader commands. Raft-family protocols detect crashed leaders via heartbeat timeout but assume that a live leader issues correct decisions; this assumption holds in log-replication systems (where entries are deterministically verifiable) but breaks in domains with partial observability and no ground-truth oracle.

Autonomous vehicle platoons, federated learning coordinators, and multi-drone survey missions all share this structure: a coordinator issues task assignments that followers cannot fully verify against a known-correct answer. A platoon leader whose perception is degraded by sensor fouling, or a federated learning aggregator subtly poisoning gradient updates, would remain undetected by heartbeat monitoring. REIP's governance architecture---proactive per-follower verification against local knowledge, confidence-weighted suspicion accumulation, and democratic impeachment---provides a lightweight template for these domains, demonstrated here on hardware as constrained as a Raspberry Pi Zero~2W (\$15, 512\,MB RAM).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=========================================================================

This paper introduced REIP, a trust-based governance framework that enables multi-robot teams to detect, impeach, and replace compromised leaders without centralized oversight or ground-truth oracle access.

The core mechanism is a proactive three-tier trust model that verifies leader commands \emph{before execution} against local knowledge ranked by source reliability. This yields a closed-form detection bound of $\lceil \sigma / (w_{\text{tier}} - r) \rceil$ commands---2~under ground-truth evidence ($w_1{=}1.0$), 8~under peer reports ($w_3{=}0.3$)---enabling sub-second fault detection without wasting physical movement on bad commands.

In multiroom experiments ($N{=}100$ per condition), REIP achieves:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $100\%$ median coverage under both clean and adversarial conditions---fault-invariant performance;
    \item Median fault detection in $0.20\,$s, impeachment in $1.21\,$s (bad-leader) and $1.67\,$s (freeze-leader), with $97{-}99\%$ detection rate;
    \item $+24.5\,$pp mean coverage advantage over Raft under Byzantine Assignment ($90.9\%$ vs.\ $66.4\%$) and $+32.2\,$pp under Freeze Leader ($96.1\%$ vs.\ $63.9\%$);
    \item Survival of two sequential Byzantine compromises in a single trial;
    \item Ablation confirmation that removing the trust model collapses coverage to $39.7\%$, confirming it is the primary mechanism.
\end{itemize}

REIP addresses a failure mode that existing approaches cannot: a leader that is alive, communicating, and issuing degraded commands. The state-of-the-art Raft protocol detects only crash failures via heartbeat timeout. Decentralized exploration avoids the problem by abandoning coordination entirely. REIP preserves the coordination advantage of hierarchical leadership while making that advantage resilient to arbitrary leadership faults through rapid, principled detection and democratic recovery.
